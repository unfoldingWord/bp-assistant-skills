# writing-symlanguage

## Purpose
Identify symbolic language where concrete images (animals, objects, numbers) represent abstract concepts, most common in prophecy, visions, and dreams.

## Definition

**Symbolic language** uses concrete images (animals, objects, numbers, places) to represent abstract concepts (kingdoms, spiritual realities, judgment). The key characteristic is that the symbol is not immediately understood without explanation - often the meaning is revealed later in the text or remains mysterious.

Key aspects:
- Symbols represent something other than their literal meaning
- Often occurs in visions, dreams, and apocalyptic literature
- The meaning may be explained in the text or require interpretation
- Symbols should be preserved in translation (not explained away)

## Distinction from Related Issues

### NOT translate-symaction (Symbolic Action)
- **symaction**: A PERSON DOES something with symbolic meaning (bowing, raising hand, falling at feet)
- **symlanguage**: A TEXT DESCRIBES something symbolic (animals, objects, numbers representing kingdoms, spirits, etc.)

| Text | Issue Type | Reason |
|------|------------|--------|
| "fell at Jesus' feet" | symaction | person's action shows respect |
| "the seven lampstands are the seven churches" | symlanguage | image represents something else |
| "raised his right hand to heaven" | symaction | gesture shows oath-taking |
| "a fourth animal with iron teeth" | symlanguage | vision image represents a kingdom |

### NOT figs-metaphor
- **metaphor**: Comparison where A is spoken of AS IF it were B (with Topic, Image, Point of Comparison)
- **symlanguage**: A represents B (meaning may be hidden or explained later; often in visions/dreams)

Symbolic language is often used in apocalyptic literature where the meaning is explicitly stated later in the text. Metaphor is a figure of speech used throughout all genres.

| Text | Issue Type | Reason |
|------|------------|--------|
| "you are the salt of the earth" | metaphor | disciples compared to salt |
| "the seven stars are the angels of the seven churches" | symlanguage | vision symbol explained |

## Recognition Process

```
Does the text contain an image that represents something else?
  |
  +-- Is it a person performing an action with cultural meaning?
  |     |
  |     YES --> translate-symaction (not symlanguage)
  |
  +-- Is it in a vision, dream, or apocalyptic context?
  |     |
  |     YES --> Likely writing-symlanguage
  |
  +-- Is the image explicitly identified as representing something?
  |     |
  |     YES ("X is Y" / "X represents Y") --> writing-symlanguage
  |
  +-- Is the symbol's meaning explained later in the passage?
  |     |
  |     YES --> writing-symlanguage
  |
  +-- Is it a comparison made for rhetorical effect (X is like Y)?
        |
        YES --> figs-metaphor or figs-simile (not symlanguage)
```

## Common Patterns

| Pattern | Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Numbers with symbolic meaning | "the seven spirits", "ten horns" | completeness, perfection |
| Animals representing kingdoms | "a fourth animal, terrifying" | empires or powers |
| Objects with prophetic meaning | "seven golden lampstands" | seven churches |
| Places with symbolic meaning | "Babylon" | Rome or worldly opposition |
| Cross-references | "See how you translated this in [X]" | consistency with similar symbols |

## Key Distinction Summary

| Feature | writing-symlanguage | translate-symaction | figs-metaphor |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Type | Object/image represents something | Person performs action | Comparison for effect |
| Context | Visions, dreams, apocalyptic | Any narrative | Any text |
| Meaning | Often explained in text | Cultural knowledge needed | Understood by comparison |
| Example | "lampstands = churches" | "fell at feet" (shows respect) | "you are salt" |

## Notes

- This issue type is rare in published notes (only 10 instances: 8 REV, 2 1PE)
- translate-symaction is far more common (491 instances across 31 books)
- When in doubt: "Is a person doing something?" (symaction) vs "Is an image representing something?" (symlanguage)

# translate-textvariants

## Purpose
Identify places where ancient manuscripts differ in their wording and note this for translators.

## Definition
A textual variant occurs when different ancient manuscript copies of the same biblical passage have different words, phrases, or verses. Scribes copying manuscripts over centuries occasionally:
- Accidentally skipped or repeated words
- Misread similar-looking letters
- Added explanatory notes that became incorporated into the text
- Harmonized passages with parallel texts elsewhere

Modern Bibles are based on scholarly analysis of which readings are most likely original.

---

## Related Issue: Alternative Readings

**Textual Variants** (this skill) and **Alternative Readings** (`translate-alternativereadings`) are connected but distinct:

| Aspect | Textual Variants | Alternative Readings |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Scope** | Greek NT and Hebrew OT manuscripts | Hebrew OT only |
| **Issue** | Different manuscript copies have different words | Masoretic scribes marked corrections in margins |
| **Question** | "Which manuscript copy to follow?" | "How to read the Hebrew text?" |
| **Sources** | Multiple manuscript families | Written text vs marginal notes (Qere/Ketiv) |

Use **translate-textvariants** when:
- Different manuscripts have different words
- The ULT follows one reading, other manuscripts have another
- Both readings are "valid" manuscript traditions

Use **translate-alternativereadings** when:
- The Hebrew marginal note (Qere) differs from written text (Ketiv)
- The Hebrew text seems corrupted and LXX offers better reading
- A scribe recommended an alternative pronunciation or word

## Confirmed Patterns from Published Notes

From Issues Resolved:
> When there are textual variants that are arguably much more likely to be the original reading than that of the Masoretic Text, we can say that in a translate-textvariants note, and while the ULT still needs to reflect the MT, the UST can reflect the meaning of the credible variant.

### Common Variant Types

#### 1. Inclusion/Omission Variants
Some manuscripts include words or phrases that others omit:
- "will reward you" vs "will reward you in the open" (Matt 6:4, 6, 18)
- "your brothers" vs "your brothers and your sisters" (Mark 3:32)
- Addition of "the Son of God" in Mark 1:1
- Disputed verses like Matt 18:11, Mark 16:9-20, John 7:53-8:11

#### 2. Word Choice Variants
Different words in different manuscripts:
- "the Gentiles" vs "the tax collectors" (Matt 5:47)
- "the kingdom" vs "the kingdom of God" (Matt 6:33)
- "eagle" vs "angel" (Rev 8:13)
- "having had compassion" vs "having become angry" (Mark 1:41)

#### 3. Number/Form Variants
Singular vs plural, or other grammatical variations:
- "a crowd" vs "large crowds" (Matt 8:18)
- "they were saying" vs "he was saying" (Mark 6:14)
- "your servants" vs "your servant" (1 Kings 1:27)

#### 4. Name/Place Variants
Different names or locations:
- "Gerasenes" vs "Gadarenes" vs "Gergesenes" (Mark 5:1)
- "Rehoboam" vs "Abijam" (1 Kings 15:6)

## Recognition Process

1. **ULT has footnote or brackets**: Major variants are often marked in the ULT with footnotes or square brackets (e.g., John 7:53-8:11)

2. **Manuscript evidence differs**: When checking source texts, you find different ancient manuscripts have different readings

3. **Other translations differ**: When comparing translations, you notice some include words/verses others omit

4. **Scholarly notes indicate variant**: Textual apparatus in critical editions marks the variation

---

## Examples from Published Notes

### NT - Word Inclusion (Matthew 5:11)
> Many ancient manuscripts include **lying**. The ULT follows that reading. Other ancient manuscripts do not include the word.

### NT - Word Choice (Revelation 15:3)
> Some ancient manuscripts read **King of the Ages**. ULT follows that reading. Other ancient manuscripts read "King of the Nations."

### OT - Word Choice (1 Kings 6:34)
> Some ancient manuscripts read "curtains." The ULT follows that reading. One ancient manuscript and several ancient versions read "leaves."

### OT - Different Phrasing (Genesis 4:15)
> That is what the Hebrew text has. Instead of that text, some ancient translations (such as the Septuagint) and many modern translations have: "Not so."

## NOT translate-textvariants

### Use translate-alternativereadings when:
- The Masoretic marginal reading (Qere) differs from written text (Ketiv)
- Scribes recommended a correction for theological/linguistic reasons
- The Hebrew text seems nonsensical and requires emendation

### Use translate-transliterate when:
- Explaining how to handle untranslated terms like "Hallelu Yah"

# figs-infostructure

## Purpose
Identify when word order, clause order, or information placement in the source text differs from what would be natural in many target languages.

## Definition
Information structure concerns how languages arrange sentence elements. Different languages:
- Place subject, verb, object in different orders
- Position time phrases at beginning vs end
- Put new/important information first vs last
- Order negative and positive statements differently
- Place vocatives (terms of address) in varying positions

The issue arises when the biblical text arranges information in a way that would be confusing or unnatural if directly transferred to the target language.

## Confirmed Classifications (Issues Resolved)

**Interjections = figs-infostructure**: When a writer introduces information where it would not ordinarily be expected (in the middle of a thought) for emphasis and emotive impact. Notes should suggest other ways of conveying emphasis/emotion.

## Categories

### 1. Time/Date Phrase Placement
Time expressions at end that languages may put first (or vice versa):
- "Noah was 600 years old **when the floodwaters came on the earth**"
- "I was poured out **from eternity, from the head, from the ancient times**"
- "**In the seventh month, on the seventeenth day** the ark rested"

### 2. Clause/Phrase Reordering
Elements in non-standard order for many languages:
- "**Clouds and wind but without rain** is the man who boasts falsely" (image before referent)
- "**To a mare among Pharaoh's chariots** I liken you, my darling" (comparison before subject)
- "**Length of days and years of life**, they will add to you" (object before verb)

### 3. Negative-Positive Order
"Not X, but Y" that languages may prefer as "Y, not X":
- "**not of the letter but of the Spirit**" --> "of the Spirit, not of the letter"
- "**I do not say up to seven times, but up to 77**" --> "I say up to 77, not seven"
- "**flesh and blood did not reveal this** but my Father" --> "my Father revealed this, not flesh and blood"

### 4. Condition-Consequence Order
"Result, if condition" that may need "If condition, then result":
- "**I will give you all this**, if you worship me" --> "If you worship me, I will give you all this"
- "**I will come soon**, if the Lord wills" --> "if the Lord wills, I will come soon"

### 5. Vocative/Address Placement
Terms of address (vocatives) at unexpected position:
- "**If you do not know**, most beautiful among women" --> "Most beautiful among women, if you do not know"
- "**I have come to my garden**, my sister, my bride" --> "My sister, my bride, I have come to my garden"

### 6. Modifier Ambiguity
When a phrase could modify different elements:
- "**even more** to show" - does "even more" modify "show" or something else?
- "**suddenly**, having looked around" - does "suddenly" go with looking or with what they saw?
- "**for 40 years**" - does it modify what precedes or what follows?

### 7. Speaker/Subject Before Action
Languages that name the speaker/actor after the action:
- "**They beat me; they wounded me; they lifted my shawl**, the guards of the walls" --> "The guards of the walls beat me..."
- "**I and this woman**" - languages naming others first would say "this woman and I"

### 8. Interjections
Information inserted mid-thought for emphasis (per Issues Resolved):
- Exclamations interrupting a statement
- Parenthetical emphasis within a sentence

## NOT This Issue

**Use writing-background** when: The issue is background/explanatory information, not word order

**Use figs-events** when: Events are narrated out of chronological order (event sequence, not word order)

**Use grammar-connect-time-sequential** when: The issue is clarifying sequence connectors, not reordering

**Use translate-versebridge** when: Information spanning multiple verses needs reordering (always multi-verse)

**Use grammar-connect-logic-result** when: Issue is reason-result relationship, not word placement within clause

## Recognition Checklist

Ask these questions:
1. Would translators naturally put these elements in a different order?
2. Is a phrase at the end that many languages put first (or vice versa)?
3. Is there a "not X, but Y" pattern that could be reversed?
4. Is there a condition after its consequence?
5. Is there a term of address (vocative) in an unusual position?
6. Could a modifier attach to different elements?
7. Is subject/speaker named after the action?
8. Is there an interjection disrupting normal flow for emphasis?

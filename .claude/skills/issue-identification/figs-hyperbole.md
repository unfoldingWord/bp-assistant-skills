# figs-hyperbole

## Purpose
Identify when speakers or writers use exaggeration or generalization for emphasis, not to deceive but to convey strong feeling, attitude, or the general truth of a statement.

## Definition
Hyperbole is a figure of speech using deliberate exaggeration or generalization. The speaker/writer does not expect to be taken literally but uses extreme statements to express emphasis, strong emotion, or general truth.

Key distinction from Translation Academy: The same words can express:
1. **Literal truth** - "It rains here every night" (actually true)
2. **Generalization** - "It rains here every night" (means most nights)
3. **Hyperbole** - "It rains here every night" (expresses strong feeling about frequency)

## Categories

### 1. Generalization (most common)
Words like "all," "every," "everyone," "whole," "always," "never," "no one" used where they do not mean absolutely every instance.

**Trigger words**: all, every, everyone, whole, always, continually, throughout, never, nothing, no one

**Examples**:
- "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (comprehensive education, not omniscience)
- "Everyone is looking for you" (many people, not literally everyone)
- "the whole crowd of the people" (large crowd, not every single person)
- "all the people of Jerusalem went out" (many/most, not literally all)
- "from all of the peoples" (from many nations)
- "who never left the temple" (spent much time there)
- "she does not know anything" (very ignorant, not truly nothing)
- "I pray for you always/continually" (frequently, habitually)

### 2. Extreme Statements/Exaggeration
Describing something with an unrealistic or impossible statement for emphasis.

**Examples**:
- "the log in your eye" (impossible - emphasizes large faults)
- "as numerous as the sand on the seashore" (uncountable army)
- "does not have a place where he might lay his head" (no permanent home)
- "the earth was split by their sound" (extremely loud)
- "I am about to die" from hunger (very hungry)
- "You will vomit up what you ate" (feel sick from eating)
- "I am disgusted with my life" (very upset)

### 3. Overstatement
Less extreme than exaggeration but still beyond literal truth.

**Examples**:
- "filled their houses with silver" (had much wealth)
- "the vats will overflow" (very full)
- "every male in Edom" killed (as many as could be found)
- "the earth was to him" (owned much land)
- "love death" (live in ways that lead to death)
- "hates his son" (acts unlovingly toward)

## Recognition Patterns

### Look for:
1. **Universalizing words**: all, every, everyone, whole, throughout, always, never, nothing, no one
2. **Impossible/unrealistic descriptions**: log in eye, sand on seashore, sky-splitting sounds
3. **Emotional emphasis in context**: complaints, boasts, proclamations, prophecies
4. **Strong statements about quantity, frequency, or scope**
5. **Common expressions that stretch reality**: "I will die," "filled with," "overflow"

### Context clues:
- Speaker expressing strong emotion (fear, frustration, amazement, urgency)
- Poetic/prophetic language
- Descriptions of large groups, armies, crowds
- Statements about prayer frequency, devotion, scope of knowledge

## NOT This Issue

**Use figs-simile instead** when:
- Explicit comparison using "like/as" with exaggeration element
- "as numerous as the sand" is hyperbole (comparing army size to sand)
- But "like the sand" combined with metaphor analysis may be figs-simile

**Literal truth is NOT hyperbole**:
- "Yahweh is righteous in all his ways" - completely true of God
- "Jesus walking on the sea" - miracle, not exaggeration
- The caution: Don't assume something is hyperbole just because it seems impossible (God does miracles)

**Use figs-merism instead** when:
- Two extremes name a totality (young and old = everyone)

**Use translate-numbers instead** when:
- The issue is about large numbers themselves, not exaggeration

## Application

### When to write a note:
- The statement would be false if taken literally
- Readers might misunderstand as a lie or error
- The emphasis/emotion might not transfer to other languages

### Template selection:
- **generalization**: For "all/every/whole/always/never" softening
- **exaggeration**: For extreme/impossible statements
- **overstatement**: For stretching truth without impossibility

### Alternate translations typically:
- Replace "all" with "many," "most," "throughout"
- Replace "every" with "many different kinds of," "various"
- Replace "always/continually" with "frequently," "regularly"
- Replace "never" with "rarely," "hardly ever"
- Replace impossible descriptions with emphatic plain language

## Genre Distribution

Hyperbole appears across all genres:
- **Narrative**: Crowd sizes, army numbers, emotional reactions
- **Poetry/Wisdom**: Proverbs, Job's complaints, prophetic oracles
- **Gospels**: Jesus' teaching (log/speck), crowd descriptions
- **Epistles**: Paul's prayer descriptions, scope of gospel reach
- **Prophecy**: Apocalyptic imagery, cosmic disturbances

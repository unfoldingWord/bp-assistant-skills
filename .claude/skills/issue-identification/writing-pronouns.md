# writing-pronouns

## Definition

Pronouns (he, she, it, they, him, her, them, etc.) are words that stand in place of nouns. The writing-pronouns issue addresses situations where:
1. **Pronoun referent is unclear** - Readers may not know who/what the pronoun refers to
2. **Independent pronouns for emphasis** - Hebrew/Greek states pronoun separately even though verb already marks it (e.g., "I, I will be king")
3. **Indefinite pronouns** - "They" used generally without specific referent

## When to Use writing-pronouns

### 1. Clarifying Unclear Referents
When multiple people are mentioned and pronouns could refer to any of them:
- "Then **he** said to **him**" (which person is which?)
- "And **she** would provoke **her**" (two women in context)
- "When **he** mentioned the Box, **he** fell" (different people)

### 2. Independent Pronouns for Emphasis (ISSUES RESOLVED DECISION)
When author/speaker states pronoun separately from verb for emphasis:
- Hebrew: "I, I will be king" (not "I myself will be king")
- Shows personal commitment, contrast with others, or emphasis
- Use writing-pronouns, NOT figs-rpronouns

Subtypes:
- **Referent focus**: "I, I will cut a covenant with you" = personal commitment
- **Contrast**: "I will set rafts... you will lift" = distinguishing roles
- **Emphasis**: "You, you know" = "You know quite well"

### 3. Indefinite Pronouns
When "they" doesn't refer to specific people:
- "They told Saul" = "Someone told Saul" / "Saul was told"
- "They burned the fat" = "the fat was burned"
- "They will call him" = "people will call him"

### 4. Pronoun Switches or Patterns
When pronouns shift in unexpected ways:
- Chapter 5 of Genesis: consistent "he...he" pattern needs clarification
- Alternating between singular/plural for collective subjects

**Note:** If the shift is between grammatical persons (e.g., 3rd person to 2nd person for the same referent), use figs-123person, not writing-pronouns. writing-pronouns is for unclear referents, emphasis, and indefinite usage — not person shifts.

## NOT writing-pronouns

| Situation | Use Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| Reflexive pronoun (himself, themselves) where same person is subject and object | figs-rpronouns |
| Singular "you" addressing a crowd | figs-youcrowd |
| Clarifying singular vs. plural "you" | figs-yousingular |
| Formal vs. informal "you" | figs-youformal |
| Exclusive/inclusive "we" | figs-exclusive |
| Speaker using third person for self | figs-123person |
| Main character introduced with name vs. pronoun | writing-participants |

## Recognition Patterns

### Pattern A: Multiple Participants
Look for verses with multiple people where pronouns appear:
- "And **he** said to **him**" - who said to whom?
- "**His** mentioning... **he** fell" - different referents

### Pattern B: Independent Pronoun Construction
Hebrew/Greek doubles the pronoun:
- Subject pronoun stated separately from verb
- Often translated as "I, I..." or "you, you..."
- Indicates emphasis, contrast, or personal commitment

### Pattern C: General/Indefinite "They"
The pronoun doesn't point to specific people:
- Often passive in meaning ("they buried him" = "he was buried")
- Context shows no specific agents identified

## Practical Examples

### Unclear Referent
> "And **he** said to **him**" (2 kings present)
- Note: "The pronoun **he** refers to Benaiah, while the pronoun **him** refers to Joab."

### Independent Pronoun
> "You, you know" (Hebrew adds separate pronoun)
- Note: "See how you translated the similar expression 'you, you swore' in [1:17]. Alternate translation: [You know quite well]"

### Indefinite They
> "And they declared to the king"
- Note: "Here the author is using the pronoun **they** in a general sense. Alternate translation: [And the king was told]"

### Genealogy Pattern
> "After he fathered Enosh, Seth lived"
- Note: "Make sure it is clear in your translation whom each pronoun refers to. Alternate translation: [After Enosh was born, Seth lived]"

## Restraint Principle — Less Is More

**Only flag a pronoun when a translator reading the verse in context would genuinely struggle to identify the referent.** Most pronouns are obvious — flag the exceptions, not the norm.

Do **NOT** flag when:
- Only one person/entity is active in the passage and the pronoun clearly refers to them
- The referent was explicitly named in the same verse or the immediately preceding verse and no other candidate intervenes
- The pronoun is a possessive ("his sword") where the possessor is the obvious subject of the sentence
- The passage is addressing God/Yahweh and "he/him" clearly refers to God throughout
- A psalm or prayer has a consistent speaker (David) and subject (God or the wicked) — pronouns alternate predictably between these two parties

**Target**: Aim for roughly 2–5 writing-pronouns notes per chapter for typical narrative/poetry. If you find yourself flagging 8+ per chapter, you are almost certainly over-flagging. Step back and ask: "Would a careful translator actually be confused here?"

## Decision Process

```
Is there a pronoun (he/she/it/they/him/her/them/etc.)?
    |
    +-- Is the referent genuinely ambiguous? (multiple plausible candidates, no recent naming)
    |   |
    |   +-- YES --> Could a careful reader still figure it out from context?
    |       |
    |       +-- NO --> Write clarifying note
    |       |
    |       +-- YES --> No note needed
    |
    +-- Is there an independent/doubled pronoun?
    |   |
    |   +-- "I, I will..." or "you, you know" pattern?
    |       |
    |       +-- YES --> Write emphasis note (NOT figs-rpronouns)
    |
    +-- Is "they" used without specific referent?
        |
        +-- YES --> Write indefinite pronoun note
```

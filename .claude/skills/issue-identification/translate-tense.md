# translate-tense

## Purpose
Identify instances where verb tenses are used irregularly to create special effects in biblical narrative.

## Definition (from Translation Academy)
**Irregular use of tenses** occurs when speakers or writers use verb tenses in ways other than marking when events happen, typically to draw special attention to what they are saying.

The Translation Academy identifies THREE types:
1. **Past for Future** - "Predictive past" / "Prophetic perfect"
2. **Present for Past** - "Historical present"
3. **Present for Future** - "Imminent future"

> "Since the aspect (perfect vs. imperfect) varies in poetry, we do not need to represent perfects with the English past tense and then write **translate-tense** or **figs-pastforfuture** notes suggesting a future-tense or habitual-present-tense AT. **Implication**: In poetry, use the appropriate tense in ULT and SKIP these notes.

## The Three Types of Irregular Tense

### 1. Past for Future ("Predictive Past" / "Prophetic Perfect")

**Definition**: Past tense used for future events to emphasize certainty.

**When to use**: Prophecy, divine promises, emphatic statements about the future.

**Examples**:
- Isaiah 5:13: "my people **have gone** into captivity" (future exile)
- Joshua 6:2: "I **have delivered** Jericho into your hand" (future conquest)
- James 5:2-3: "Your riches **have rotted**" (future decay)

**Note**: For this type, figs-pastforfuture may be preferred (more specific).

---

### 2. Present for Past ("Historical Present")

**Definition**: Present tense used in past narration to make events more vivid, as if happening now.

**When to use**: Narrative passages, especially to:
- Call attention to a development in the story
- Make direct speech stand out from narration
- Set the scene for a new set of events
- Draw the reader into the story

**Examples from Mark**:
- Mark 1:30: "immediately they **speak** to him concerning her" (past event narrated in present)
- Mark 1:36-38: "they **found** him and **say** to him" (mixed tenses for narrative impact)
- Mark 1:21: "he **enters** into Capernaum" (scene-setting)

**Translation Strategy**: Use past tense if present-in-past-narration is unnatural in your language.

---

### 3. Present for Future ("Imminent Future")

**Definition**: Present tense used for future events that will happen soon or are certain.

**When to use**: Statements about immediate or certain future events.

**Examples**:
- Exodus 11:4: "I **am going out** in the midst of Egypt" (imminent action)
- Habakkuk 1:6: "behold me, **raising up** the Chaldeans" (certain future)
- Revelation 2:5: "I **am coming** to you" (certain warning)
- 1 Samuel 3:11: "I **am judging** his house" (emphatic future)

**Translation Strategy**: Use future tense or "about to" form if present-for-future is unclear.

## Examples from Published Notes

### Mark (most extensive use):

**Mark 1:7** (present for future):
> "**is coming**" - Present for certain future
> Note: "Here John uses the present tense to refer to something that will certainly happen in the future."

**Mark 1:8** (past tense ambiguity):
> "**baptized**" - Could be general/habitual OR specific past action
> Note: "Here John could be using the past tense: (1) to refer generally to how he baptizes people... (2) to refer specifically to how he just baptized some people."

**Mark 9:39** (future for general truth):
> "**will do... will be able**" - Future tense for general truth
> Note: "Here Jesus uses the future tense to describe something that is generally true."

**Mark 10:7** (future for general truth):
> "**will leave**" - Future for general statement
> Note: "Here Jesus uses the future tense to speak about something that is generally true."

**Mark 11:3** (present for imminent future):
> "**he sends**" - Present for soon-to-happen
> Note: "Here Jesus uses the present tense to describe a future action that will happen very soon."

### 1 Samuel:

**1 Samuel 14:10**:
> "Yahweh **has given** them into our hand"
> Note: "Jonathan is using the past tense to describe something that he believes would certainly happen in the future."

**1 Samuel 25:26**:
> "who **has restrained** you"
> Note: "Abigail is using the past tense to describe something that she believes is happening in the present."

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify tense mismatch**: Does the verb tense not match the time of the event?

2. **Determine the direction**:
   - Past tense + Future event = Past for Future
   - Present tense + Past event = Present for Past (Historical Present)
   - Present tense + Future event = Present for Future (Imminent Future)
   - Future tense + General truth = Future for General Statement

3. **Check if poetry**: If in poetic text with varying aspect:
   - Per Issues Resolved, use appropriate tense in ULT
   - No note needed

4. **Choose the issue type**:
   - Past for Future: Consider figs-pastforfuture (more specific)
   - Other directions: Use translate-tense

5. **Select appropriate template** from the three available.

## When to Use translate-tense vs figs-pastforfuture

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|----------------|
| Prophecy with past-for-future | Either works; figs-pastforfuture more specific |
| Divine promise with past-for-future | Either works; figs-pastforfuture more specific |
| Historical present (present-for-past) | translate-tense only |
| Imminent future (present-for-future) | translate-tense only |
| Future for general truth | translate-tense only |
| Poetry with aspect variation | No note needed (per Issues Resolved) |

---

## Special Cases

### General/Habitual Statements
Sometimes past or future tense is used for general truths:
- "A man **will leave** his father" (general principle, not specific future)
- Use translate-tense to suggest general present tense

### Mixed Tenses in Narration
Authors may mix tenses for effect:
- Mark frequently shifts between past and present
- Note the pattern in chapter intro if extensive

### Poetry and Psalms
Per Issues Resolved, do NOT write translate-tense or figs-pastforfuture notes for:
- Hebrew perfects in poetry
- Habitual present-tense statements
- General statements
Instead, use the appropriate English tense in the ULT.

# figs-reduplication

## Purpose
Identify repeated words or word-forms used for emphasis or intensification.

## Definition
Reduplication is the repetition of a word or related forms of a word for emphasis or intensification. The most common form in Hebrew is the **infinitive absolute + finite verb** construction (e.g., "dying, you shall die"), but it also includes any deliberate repetition used to intensify meaning.

---

## The Key Distinction: figs-reduplication vs writing-poetry (Cognate Accusative)

| figs-reduplication | writing-poetry (cognate accusative) |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Same verb repeated** twice | **Verb + noun** from same root |
| "dying, he shall die" | "he dreamed a dream" |
| "knowing, you know" | "any sin that he sins" |
| "hearing, hear" | "he shouted a great shout" |
| Verbal emphasis only | Verb + object relationship |

**From Issues Resolved**: "The template that we're using for cognate accusative (verb and object from same root) is writing-poetry, because it's a topic that is explicitly mentioned in there. Ex: 'he dreamed a dream,' 'any sin that he sins,' 'he shouted a great shout,' 'sacrificing sacrifices'"

## Types of Reduplication

### 1. Infinitive Absolute + Finite Verb (Hebrew)
The most common pattern: an infinitive absolute immediately before its corresponding finite verb.

| ULT Pattern | Meaning | Example AT |
|-------------|---------|------------|
| "dying, you shall die" | certainty of death | "you shall certainly die" |
| "knowing, you know" | definite knowledge | "you certainly know" |
| "giving, we will give" | firm commitment | "we will certainly give" |
| "returning, return" | emphatic command | "be careful to return" |
| "blessing, he blessed" | intensity of blessing | "he greatly blessed" |

Recognition: Look for verb forms like "Verb-ing, verb" or "verb, [subject] verb-ed" in ULT.

### 2. Repeated Words for Emphasis
Direct repetition of the same word to intensify its meaning.

| ULT Pattern | Example | AT |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| "near, near" | Zeph 1:14 | "very near" |
| "haughty, haughty" | 1 Sam 2:3 | "you very haughty person" |
| "Woe, woe, woe" | Rev 8:13 | "What great woe" |
| "Bad! Bad!" | Prov 20:14 | "It is really bad quality!" |
| "In the morning, in the morning" | Zeph 3:5 | "Every morning" |

### 3. Repeated Names/Addresses (Urgency/Emotion)
Names repeated to express deep feeling or urgency.

| Pattern | Context | AT |
|---------|---------|-----|
| "Lord, Lord" | Matt 7:21-22 | "My Lord!" or repeated for urgency |
| "Martha, Martha" | Luke 10:41 | "My dear Martha" |
| "My God, my God" | Mark 15:34 | "Please, my God" (urgency) |

### 4. Repeated Pronouns (Emphasis)
Hebrew uses independently stated pronouns with verbs for contrast or emphasis.

| Pattern | Effect | Note |
|---------|--------|------|
| "I, I will" | personal commitment | Often figs-reduplication |
| "upon him, him also" | emphasis on person | "even upon him" |
| "going, he went" | continuous action | "he kept going" |

## Recognition Process

1. **Look for verbal repetition**: Is the same verb (or verbal root) appearing twice in close proximity?

2. **Check the pattern**:
   - Infinitive + finite verb ("dying, he died") -> figs-reduplication
   - Verb + noun from same root ("dreamed a dream") -> writing-poetry (cognate accusative)

3. **Check for word repetition**: Is a word/phrase repeated immediately for emphasis?

4. **Check for name repetition**: Is a name or address repeated to show emotion/urgency?

5. **Determine the effect**: What is being intensified?
   - Certainty ("certainly," "surely")
   - Intensity ("very," "greatly," "completely")
   - Urgency (earnest request, emotion)
   - Continuous action ("kept doing," "continually")

## NOT figs-reduplication

### writing-poetry (Cognate Accusative)
Verb followed by a noun from the same root:
- "he dreamed a dream" -> writing-poetry
- "sacrificing sacrifices" -> writing-poetry
- "any sin that he sins" -> writing-poetry
- "lamented this lamentation" -> writing-poetry (NOT reduplication or explicitinfo)

Per Issues Resolved: cognate accusative (verb + noun from same root) uses writing-poetry.

### figs-parallelism
Two different clauses saying the same thing:
- "Praise Yahweh; exalt him" -> figs-parallelism (different verbs)
- "dying, you shall die" -> figs-reduplication (same verb repeated)

### figs-doublet
Two different synonymous words joined:
- "scattered and dispersed" -> figs-doublet (different words)
- "near, near" -> figs-reduplication (same word repeated)

### Simple Clarification (No Note Needed)
Some repetition clarifies rather than intensifies:
- 1 Sam 20:26: "not clean, surely not clean" - shows Saul talking himself into a conclusion

## Common AT Patterns

| Source Pattern | AT Options |
|---------------|------------|
| "Verb-ing, verb" | "certainly [verb]," "surely [verb]," "[verb] indeed" |
| Word repeated twice | "very [word]," "[word] completely," "[word] indeed" |
| Name repeated | "My dear [name]," "Please, [name]" |
| Continuous action | "kept [verb]-ing," "[verb]-ed continually" |
| Strong command | "Be sure to [verb]," "It is urgent that you [verb]" |

## Genre Considerations

### Hebrew Narrative (Genesis, Samuel, Kings, etc.)
- Infinitive absolute construction is common
- Often indicates oaths, certainty, or intensity
- "You shall surely die" in commands/warnings

### Hebrew Poetry (Psalms, Job, Proverbs)
- Word repetition for poetic effect
- "Hear, hear my words" for urgency
- "Pity me, pity me" for emotional appeal

### Prophets (Zephaniah, Joel, etc.)
- Word repetition for urgency of message
- "Near, near" emphasizing imminence
- Commands with emphatic force

### NT (Gospels, Revelation)
- Name repetition for emotion ("Martha, Martha")
- "Lord, Lord" showing urgency
- "Woe, woe" for emphasis in judgments

# writing-endofstory

## Purpose
Identify concluding information that signals the end of a story, episode, or section.

## Definition
End-of-story information is background material that marks the conclusion of a narrative unit. Unlike main storyline events, this material wraps up or comments on what has just happened. Different types include:

1. **Summary** - Restating the main point of the story
2. **Comment** - Author's reflection on what happened
3. **Connection** - Linking a smaller story to the larger narrative
4. **Character resolution** - What happens to specific characters after the main events
5. **Ongoing action** - Actions that continue after the story ends
6. **Result** - What happened as a consequence of the story's events

## Recognition Patterns

### Common Markers

**Hebrew narrative:**
- "And it happened that..." (wayyehi) at story conclusions
- Perfect tense verbs summarizing completed events
- Summary statements after detailed narratives
- "And he judged Israel X years" type conclusions

**Greek narrative (especially in Matthew):**
- kai egeneto hote etelesen ("And it happened that when [Jesus] finished...")
- Summary statements about teaching or preaching
- Departure statements following teaching sections

**NT Epistles:**
- Closing sections with final greetings
- Summary statements at letter conclusions
- Transitional phrases like "as to the rest" (to loipon)

### Content Signals

1. **Departure after events**: Character leaves the scene
2. **Duration summaries**: "He judged Israel 40 years"
3. **Fame/report spreading**: "A report about him went out"
4. **Reaction summaries**: "The crowds were astonished"
5. **Teaching conclusions**: "When Jesus finished speaking..."
6. **Final comments**: Author's closing observations

## Examples from Published Notes

### Summary Statement
> "So David was stronger than the Philistine with the sling and with the stone" (1 Sam 17:50)
- Concluding summary of David vs. Goliath fight

### What Happened as Result
> "And a report about him went out" (Luke 4:37)
- Result of the events within the story

### Character Resolution
> "And he had judged Israel 40 years" (1 Sam 4:18)
- Final mention of Eli in the book

### Ending an Episode
> "Then Saul went up from after the Philistines. And the Philistines went to their place" (1 Sam 14:46)
- Concluding the battle story

### Teaching Conclusion (Matthew pattern)
> "And it happened that when Jesus finished speaking these words, the crowds were astonished at his teaching" (Matt 7:28)
- Matthew uses this formula repeatedly (7:28, 11:1, 13:53, 19:1, 26:1)

### Book/Gospel Conclusion
> "In 20:30-31 John comments about the story he has written... He does this in order to indicate that the story is almost finished." (John 20:30-31)

### Account Conclusion (non-narrative)
> "Like this he made the ten stands" (1 Kings 7:37)
- Concluding a descriptive account of craftsmanship

### Letter Conclusion
> "As to the rest..." (Phil 4:8)
- Marking the closing section of an epistle

## NOT This Issue

| Use Instead | When |
|-------------|------|
| writing-background | General background info that does NOT conclude a story |
| writing-newevent | Introducing a NEW episode (not ending one) |
| writing-participants | Introducing characters (not their final mention) |
| figs-explicit | Reader needs unstated information (not story structure) |

## Key Distinction: writing-endofstory vs writing-background

| writing-endofstory | writing-background |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Signals conclusion of a narrative unit | Provides context during/before main events |
| Comes AFTER the main action | Can come before or during main action |
| Wraps up, summarizes, or comments on story | Sets up or explains what follows |
| "And he judged Israel 40 years" (final) | "Now Eli was very old" (setting up events) |
| "A report about him went out" (result) | "Now it was the Sabbath" (circumstance) |

## Note Patterns in Published TNs

1. **Result/consequence**: "This is a comment about what happened after the story as a result of the events within the story itself."

2. **Story conclusion**: "Here the author is presenting information that concludes the story of [EVENT/CHARACTER]."

3. **Episode ending**: "Here the author is concluding the story of [EVENT]. Your language may have its own way of ending an episode of a story."

4. **Teaching conclusion**: "This sentence marks the end of Jesus' [sermon/teaching] by [describing reaction/summarizing/stating what he did next]."

5. **Summary**: "Here the author is offering a concluding summary of [EVENT]."

6. **Book/section conclusion**: "Use the natural form in your language for expressing the conclusion of a story."

## Recognition Process

```
Is this material that wraps up or concludes a narrative section?
  |
  +-- Does it come AFTER the main events of a story/episode?
  |     |
  |     +-- YES: Does it summarize, comment on, or describe what happened next?
  |     |         |
  |     |         +-- YES --> writing-endofstory
  |     |         +-- NO: Is it introducing a NEW story? --> writing-newevent
  |     |
  |     +-- NO: Is it setting up or explaining events to come?
  |               --> writing-background
  |
  +-- Is it marking a transition to a new section?
        |
        +-- Ending current section --> writing-endofstory
        +-- Beginning new section --> writing-newevent
```

## Purposes of End-of-Story Information (from TA)

1. To summarize the story
2. To give a comment about what happened in the story
3. To connect a smaller story to the larger story it is part of
4. To tell what happens to a specific character after the main part of the story ends
5. To tell of ongoing action that continues after the main part ends
6. To tell what happens after the story as a result of events in the story itself

## Languages Mark Conclusions Differently

End-of-story information may be marked by:
- Specific concluding formulas
- Changes in verb tense or aspect
- Summary particles or conjunctions
- Discourse markers
- Changes in sentence structure

The note should remind translators to use whatever natural form their language has for expressing story conclusions.

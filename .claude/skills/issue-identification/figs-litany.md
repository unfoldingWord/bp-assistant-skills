# figs-litany

## Purpose
Identify litanies (repetitive series of similar statements used to communicate comprehensiveness).

## Definition
A litany is a figure of speech where various components of a thing are listed in a **series of very similar statements**. The speaker uses this structure to indicate that what he is saying should be understood as **comprehensive and without exceptions**.

Key characteristics:
- **Three or more** similar clauses/statements
- **Repetitive structure** - same grammatical pattern repeated
- **Comprehensive intent** - the list is meant to be exhaustive or overwhelming
- Often spans multiple verses

---

## Litany vs Similar Figures

### Litany vs Parallelism

| Litany | Parallelism |
|--------|-------------|
| **3+ similar clauses** in a series | Usually **2 clauses** |
| Lists components exhaustively | Repeats same idea for emphasis |
| "Though they dig... Though they climb... Though they hide..." | "Praise Yahweh / exalt him" |
| Shows comprehensiveness | Shows intensification |

### Litany vs Doublet

| Litany | Doublet |
|--------|---------|
| **Series of clauses** | **Two words/phrases** |
| Extended list (often multi-verse) | Brief, usually within one phrase |
| "iniquity, transgression, and sin" could be doublet/extended doublet, but when each item has its own clause structure, it's litany | "blameless and righteous" |

## Common Litany Patterns

### 1. Conditional Series ("Though/If" pattern)
Repeated conditional clauses showing no escape:
- "Though they dig into Sheol... Though they climb up to heaven... Though they hide on the top of Carmel... Though they are hidden in the bottom of the sea... Though they go into captivity..." (Amos 9:2-4)

### 2. Prohibition Series ("You should not have" pattern)
Repeated negative statements emphasizing comprehensive failure:
- "You should not have looked... You should not have rejoiced... You should not have made your mouth great... You should not have entered... You should not have looted... You should not have stood... You should not have delivered up..." (Obadiah 1:12-14)

### 3. Judgment Series
Repeated descriptions of destruction/judgment:
- Series in Zephaniah 1 listing what will be cut off
- Series in Joel describing locust devastation

### 4. List of Sins/Wrongs
Extended enumeration of offenses:
- "iniquity, transgression, and sin" (Exodus 34:7) - every kind of wrong
- Lists in prophetic judgment passages

### 5. Descriptive Series
Participial or descriptive phrases building a complete picture:
- Job 26:7-9 description of God's power

---

## Recognition Process

1. **Count the parallel elements**: Is this 3+ similar statements, not just 2?
   - 2 clauses -> likely parallelism
   - 3+ similar clauses -> consider litany

2. **Check for repetitive structure**: Is the same grammatical pattern repeated?
   - "Though X... Though Y... Though Z..."
   - "You should not have X... You should not have Y..."

3. **Assess the intent**: Is the speaker trying to show comprehensiveness?
   - Exhaustive list of possibilities (no escape)
   - Complete enumeration (every kind of sin)
   - Overwhelming accumulation (total judgment)

4. **Check the span**: Litanies often extend across multiple verses

---

## NOT figs-litany

### Use figs-parallelism when:
Only two clauses are parallel (standard poetic parallelism):
- "The heavens declare the glory of God / the skies proclaim the work of his hands"

### Use figs-doublet when:
Two or three synonymous **words** (not full clauses):
- "iniquity and transgression" at word level

### Use figs-merism when:
Two extremes represent totality (not a list):
- "young and old" = everyone
- "heaven and earth" = all creation

---

## Translation Strategies from TA

If the litany is understood naturally, translate as-is. If not:

1. **Use a summary statement**: Often there's a general statement at the beginning or end that sums up the litany's meaning - format this to show it's a summary.

2. **Format for clarity**: Put each sentence on a separate line; if each sentence has two parts, line up equivalent parts visually.

3. **Remove connecting words**: Eliminate "and," "but," "or" at sentence beginnings to clarify that items are being listed in sequence.

## Genre Considerations

### Prophetic Books (Primary location)
Litany is most common in prophetic judgment oracles:
- Amos 9:2-4 (no escape from judgment)
- Obadiah 1:12-14 (comprehensive accusation)
- Zephaniah 1:4-6 (complete removal)
- Joel (locust devastation)

### Poetry (Job, Psalms)
Extended descriptions of God's power or comprehensive statements.

### Law (Exodus, Leviticus)
Lists of sins or comprehensive statements about forgiveness.

### Apocalyptic (Revelation)
Series of judgments or descriptions.

## Biblical Examples

### Amos 9:2-4 (Classic No-Escape Litany)
```
Though they dig into Sheol, there my hand will take them.
Though they climb up to heaven, there I will bring them down.
Though they hide on the top of Carmel, there I will search and take them.
Though they are hidden from my sight in the bottom of the sea,
    there will I give orders to the serpent, and it will bite them.
Though they go into captivity, driven by their enemies before them,
    there will I give orders to the sword, and it will kill them.
```
Point: No matter where they go, they cannot escape judgment.

### Obadiah 1:12-14 (Accusation Litany)
Eight "You should not have" statements accusing Edom of failure to help Judah.
Point: Comprehensive failure across every opportunity.

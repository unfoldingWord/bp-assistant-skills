# writing-proverbs

## Purpose
Identify short sayings that give wise advice or teach something generally true about life.

## Definition
A proverb is a short saying that gives wise advice or teaches something generally true about life. Proverbs express much meaning in few words, often using metaphor, parallelism, or comparison.

## Recognition Criteria

Mark as writing-proverbs when:

1. **Short general statement about life** - A concise saying presenting wisdom applicable beyond the immediate context
2. **Speaker uses/invents a saying** - Explicit or implicit indication that the speaker is quoting or creating a popular saying
3. **Form indicators** - Compact structure, balanced phrasing, memorable wording
4. **Teaching purpose** - Statement meant to illustrate, prove, or apply a broader truth

## Categories

### Teaching Proverbs
Jesus or speakers using short sayings to teach principles:
- "With the measure you measure, it will be measured to you" (Matt 7:2)
- "A disciple is not above his teacher" (Matt 10:24)
- "The laborer is worthy of his food" (Matt 10:10)

### Applied Quotations
Existing cultural sayings applied to specific situations:
- "Doctor, heal yourself" (Luke 4:23)
- "No prophet is accepted in his hometown" (Luke 4:24)
- "Is Saul also among the prophets?" (1 Sam 10:12)

### Figurative Comparison Proverbs
Using metaphor/comparison to teach:
- "The healthy do not need a physician, but the sick" (Matt 9:12, Mark 2:17, Luke 5:31)
- "Every kingdom divided against itself is made desolate" (Matt 12:25)
- "A little leaven leavens the whole lump" (Gal 5:9)

### Enigmatic/Cryptic Proverbs
Meaning uncertain or open to interpretation:
- "Where the body is, there the vultures will gather" (Matt 24:28, Luke 17:37)
- "Light is near from the face of darkness" (Job 17:12)

## NOT writing-proverbs

### Use figs-parables instead
- Extended illustrative stories (kingdom parables, prodigal son)
- Stories with characters and plot development

### Use figs-idiom instead
- Fixed expressions with non-compositional meaning ("in the eyes of," "burning nose")
- Cultural phrases NOT presented as general wisdom

### Use figs-metaphor instead
- Figurative language NOT in proverb form
- Extended metaphors without proverbial structure

### Use figs-simile instead
- Simple comparisons ("like a lily," "as the sand") NOT functioning as proverbs

### Use figs-hyperbole instead
- Exaggerations for emphasis without proverbial character

## Key Distinctions

| Feature | writing-proverbs | figs-idiom | figs-parables |
|---------|------------------|------------|---------------|
| Length | Short (1-2 sentences) | Phrase/expression | Extended story |
| Purpose | Teach general truth | Express meaning | Illustrate specific point |
| Structure | Self-contained wisdom | Fixed phrase | Narrative with plot |
| Application | Generally applicable | Context-specific | Single teaching point |

## Common Contexts

1. **Gospels** - Jesus teaching disciples/crowds (most common)
2. **Wisdom Literature** - Job, Proverbs (the entire book consists of proverbs)
3. **Epistles** - Paul citing cultural sayings (1 Cor 15:33, Gal 5:9, Titus 1:15)
4. **OT Narrative** - Characters using popular sayings (1 Sam, Judges)

## Note Writing Approach

Per Issues Resolved (Apr 16, 2025): The approach for writing-proverbs is the same as translate-blessing - inform translators that the biblical speaker is using a form they might not recognize and encourage them to use a comparable form from their own language.

Standard note pattern:
1. Identify that speaker uses/invents/quotes a proverb
2. Briefly state what the proverb teaches (the general principle)
3. Note it should be translated as a recognizable proverb in the target language
4. Provide AT that preserves the proverbial sense

## Signal Phrases

Phrases suggesting proverbs:
- Uses or invents a proverb
- Quoting/creating a short popular saying
- A common expression of the culture
- A saying about something generally true in life
- This proverb teaches that...

## Recognition Flow

```
Is there a short, memorable statement teaching general wisdom?
  |
  +-- Is it 1-2 sentences with balanced/compact structure?
  |     |
  |     +-- YES: Continue checking
  |     +-- NO: Probably not a proverb
  |
  +-- Does it teach something applicable beyond immediate context?
  |     |
  |     +-- YES: Likely a proverb
  |     +-- NO: Check figs-idiom or other figures
  |
  +-- Is the speaker presenting it as wise/general teaching?
  |     |
  |     +-- YES: --> writing-proverbs
  |     +-- NO: May still be proverb if structurally clear
```

## Examples from Published Notes

**Matt 7:2** - "with the measure by which you measure, it will be measured to you"
- Teaching: People experience for themselves how they have treated others
- Pattern: Action/consequence structure

**Matt 10:24** - "A disciple is not above his teacher, nor a slave above his master"
- Teaching: Followers should expect treatment similar to their leader
- Pattern: Parallel negative statements

**Luke 4:23** - "Doctor, heal yourself"
- Teaching: Applied to Jesus proving himself in his hometown
- Pattern: Imperative form, cultural saying

**1 Cor 15:33** - "Bad company corrupts good morals"
- Teaching: Friends influence character
- Pattern: Subject/predicate wisdom statement

**2 Pet 2:22** - "A dog returns to its own vomit, and a washed pig to the rolling of mud"
- Teaching: False teachers return to sinful ways
- Pattern: Paired animal illustrations

# grammar-collectivenouns

## Purpose
Identify when singular nouns referring to groups may need translation notes because the target language may require plural forms.

## Definition
A collective noun is a singular noun that refers to a group of something. The singular form creates translation issues when:
1. The target language lacks an equivalent collective noun
2. Subject-verb agreement differs (some languages require plural verbs with collective nouns)
3. Pronoun agreement is unclear (singular noun but plural referents)
4. Hebrew uses ancestor names metonymically for descendants

## When to Use
Use when a **singular noun** refers to a **group** and the plural form would be more natural in translation.

---

## Categories

### 1. Group Names (People/Nations)
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Israel | the Israelites |
| Judah, Ephraim, Benjamin | the people of that tribe |
| the circumcision/uncircumcision | circumcised/uncircumcised people |
| race, seed (descendants) | a group of people |
| the law | group of laws/commandments |

### 2. Collective Animals/Nature
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| flock | group of sheep/goats |
| locust | swarm of locusts |
| cattle, ox | bulls and cows |
| army | soldiers as a group |
| swarm | flying insects |
| hair | many hairs |

### 3. Body Parts (Singular for Multiple)
When referring to a group of people, Hebrew often uses singular body parts:
| Term | Context |
|------|---------|
| heart | "their heart" = all their hearts |
| voice | "lifted up their voice" = voices |
| hand | "into their hand" = hands |
| foot/sandal | "his foot" = both feet |
| head | "their head" = heads |
| face | "their face" = faces |
| mouth | "to their mouth" = mouths |
| soul | "their soul" = souls/lives |
| eye | "your eye" = eyes |

### 4. Objects (Singular Representing Many)
| Term | Example |
|------|---------|
| root | "the root of the trees" = roots |
| fruit/seed | many fruits/seeds |
| wall, bar | city walls and bars |
| network | set of networks |
| table | "from the table" = tables |
| sandal | "his sandal" = both sandals |

---

## Key Patterns from Examples

### Pattern 1: Singular noun + plural verb/pronoun
"the **crowd** was coming to him, and he was teaching **them**" (Mark 2:13)
- Note needed: Noun singular but pronoun plural

### Pattern 2: Ancestor name for descendants
"**Israel**" = "the Israelites" or "the Israelite people"
- Common in OT narrative and prophecy

### Pattern 3: Body part with possessive plural
"their **heart**" when referring to multiple people
- Suggest: "their hearts" in translation

### Pattern 4: Generic singular + group meaning
Collective **man** when verb is singular: "a man of Israel was summoned"
- From Issues Resolved: Translate as "man" when verb singular, "men" when verb plural

---

## NOT grammar-collectivenouns

### Use figs-metonymy instead:
- "Israel" representing the nation's actions (association, not just quantity)
- "gates" representing people/community (not physical boundary)

### Use figs-metaphor instead:
- "house of Israel" (household metaphor for descendants)
- "seed" meaning descendants (when metaphorical, not just collective)

### Use translate-plural instead:
- Plural of excellence/intensive plural ("seas" for emphasis on greatness)
- Deliberate plural for rhetorical effect

---

## Issues Resolved Decisions

1. **ULT should represent Hebrew singular pronouns with English singular pronouns**, even if readings vary between plural and singular. Write collective nouns notes to address this.

2. **Collective "man" (Hebrew)**: Translate as "man" when verb is singular, "men" when verb is plural. Use collective nouns note. If many instances, put discussion in chapter/book introduction.

3. **"This people" with singular pronouns**: Use "he" as pronoun (matching Hebrew), write explanation in chapter intro, refer back at first instance.

## Recognition Process

```
Is there a singular noun referring to a group?
  |
  +-- Yes: Could translators use a plural noun more naturally?
      |
      +-- Yes: Is it a nation/people name (Israel, Judah)?
      |   --> grammar-collectivenouns (group name category)
      |
      +-- Yes: Is it a body part with plural possessive ("their heart")?
      |   --> grammar-collectivenouns (body part category)
      |
      +-- Yes: Is it a collective animal/nature term (flock, locust)?
      |   --> grammar-collectivenouns (animals/nature category)
      |
      +-- Yes: Is it a singular object representing many (fruit, seed)?
      |   --> grammar-collectivenouns (objects category)
      |
      +-- No: The singular form works in translation
          --> No note needed
```

---

## Note Structure

Standard form:
"In this verse, the word **[term]** is singular in form, but it refers to [all X / multiple X / a group of X]. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use the plural form of '[term].' Alternate translation: [plural form]"

Continuity reference:
"See how you translated the similar expression in [verse](../##/##.md)."

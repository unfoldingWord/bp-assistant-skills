# grammar-connect-exceptions

## Purpose
Identify exception clauses where a speaker excludes one or more items from a group, which can sound contradictory in some languages.

## Definition
An exception clause excludes one or more items or people from a group. The pattern is:
- Part 1: A general statement (often negative)
- Part 2: An exception to that statement

From Translation Academy:
> Exceptional relationship connectors exclude one or more items or people from a group... In some languages this type of construction does not make sense because the exception in Part 2 seems to contradict the statement in Part 1.

---

## Common Exception Patterns

### Pattern 1: "No X except Y" = "Only Y"
The most common pattern. A negative statement with an exception.

**Markers**: except, except for, but, other than, besides

**Examples**:
- 1 Corinthians 8:4: "there is no God **except** one"
  - AT: "there is **only** one God"

- Matthew 11:27: "no one knows the Son **except** the Father"
  - AT: "**only** the Father knows the Son"

- Matthew 24:36: "no one knows, neither the angels... **except** the Father only"
  - AT: "**only** the Father knows"

- Mark 10:18: "No one is good **except** one--God"
  - AT: "**Only** one is good--God"

- 1 Corinthians 2:11: "no one knows the things of God **except** the Spirit of God"
  - AT: "**only** the Spirit of God knows the things of God"

### Pattern 2: "Not X unless Y" = "Only X if Y"
A negative statement that becomes positive with a condition.

**Markers**: unless, if not, except if

**Examples**:
- Matthew 5:20: "**unless** your righteousness abounds more... you will **not** enter"
  - AT: "you will **only** enter **if** your righteousness abounds more"

- Matthew 18:3: "**unless** you turn and become like children, you will **not** enter"
  - AT: "you will **only** enter **if** you turn and become like children"

- Mark 7:3: "do **not** eat **unless** they wash their hands"
  - AT: "**only** eat **if** they have washed their hands"

- 1 Corinthians 15:36: "is **not** caused to live **unless** it dies"
  - AT: "is **only** caused to live **once** it dies"

### Pattern 3: "All X except Y" = "Most X, but not Y"
A positive general statement with an exception carved out.

**Markers**: except, but not, other than

**Examples**:
- Matthew 12:31: "**every** sin will be forgiven... **but** blasphemy of the Spirit will **not**"
  - AT: "the **only** sin that will not be forgiven is blasphemy of the Spirit"
  - Or: "**most** sins will be forgiven, but blasphemy of the Spirit will not"

- 1 Corinthians 6:18: "**Every** sin... is outside the body, **but** the one being sexually immoral sins against his own body"
  - AT: "**Almost every** sin... is outside the body"

### Pattern 4: "X, but only Y" (Restriction/Limitation)
A positive statement with a restriction added.

**Markers**: but only, only, as long as

**Examples**:
- 1 Corinthians 7:39: "she is free to marry whomever she wishes, **but only** in the Lord"
  - AT: "she is free to marry whomever she wishes **as long as** they are in the Lord"

- Matthew 5:32: "everyone divorcing his wife, **except for** sexual immorality, causes her to commit adultery"
  - AT: "the **only** time a man is allowed to divorce his wife is when there is sexual immorality"

### Pattern 5: "Nothing except Y" = "Only Y"
Emphasizing that something is the sole option.

**Markers**: nothing except, nothing but

**Examples**:
- Matthew 14:17: "We have **nothing** here **except** five loaves and two fish"
  - AT: "The **only** food we have here is five loaves and two fish"

- Mark 11:13: "he found **nothing except** leaves"
  - AT: "he found **only** leaves"

- Mark 6:8: "take **nothing** for the road **except only** a staff"
  - AT: "the **only** thing they should take was a staff"

## NOT grammar-connect-exceptions

### Use grammar-connect-logic-contrast for:

**Key distinction**: Both issues can use "but", but they function differently:

| Feature | grammar-connect-exceptions | grammar-connect-logic-contrast |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Pattern** | One item EXCLUDED from a group | Two things COMPARED as different |
| **"But" means** | "except" / "other than" | "however" / "in contrast" |
| **Structure** | "All X... but not Y" (Y carved out from X) | "X happened... but Y is different" |

**Examples showing the difference:**

**grammar-connect-exceptions** (1 Cor 6:18):
- "Every sin is outside the body, **but** sexual immorality sins against the body"
- Sexual immorality is EXCLUDED from the group "every sin"
- "But" = "except"

**grammar-connect-logic-contrast** (1 Cor 3:6):
- "I planted, Apollos watered, **but** God gave the growth"
- Human work is CONTRASTED with God's work (not excluded from it)
- "But" = "however"

**Quick Test**:
- Is one item being CARVED OUT from a larger group? --> grammar-connect-exceptions
- Are two things being SET AGAINST each other as different/opposite? --> grammar-connect-logic-contrast

| Expression | Why NOT exception |
|------------|-------------------|
| "I did X, but God did Y" | Contrast between actors, not exception from group |
| "They expected X, but Y happened" | Contrast between expectation and reality |
| "Before X, but now Y" | Contrast between times |

### Use grammar-connect-condition-* for:
| Expression | Why NOT exception |
|------------|-------------------|
| "if not" as a condition | Conditional relationship, not exception |
| "unless" as true condition | May be condition-fact or condition-hypothetical |

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify the structure**: Is there a general statement + exception?
   - Look for: except, unless, but not, other than, besides, only

2. **Identify what's excluded**: What is being carved out from the general group?
   - Part 1: The general statement (often negative)
   - Part 2: The exception (what's excluded from Part 1)

3. **Check for apparent contradiction**:
   - Does Part 2 seem to contradict Part 1?
   - Would readers be confused by the structure?

4. **Choose a translation strategy**:
   - Can "only" replace "no X except"?
   - Would reversing the order help?
   - Is "as long as" appropriate for restrictions?

## Linguistic Markers

### English Exception Words
- except, except for, except that
- unless
- but, but not, but only
- other than
- besides
- only (when limiting)

### Greek Markers
- ei me (if not) - common exception marker
- ean me (unless)
- plēn (except, but)
- ektos (outside of, except)

### Hebrew Markers
- ki 'im (except, unless)
- bilti (except, without)
- 'ak (only, surely)

## Summary: The Key Test

**grammar-connect-exceptions** = General statement + exception that might sound contradictory

Ask yourself:
1. Is there a general statement (often negative) with an exception?
2. Could readers think the speaker is contradicting themselves?
3. Can the meaning be preserved with "only" or by reversing the order?

If YES to these --> grammar-connect-exceptions

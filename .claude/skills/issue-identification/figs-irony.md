# figs-irony

## Purpose
Identify irony in biblical text, distinguishing it from hyperbole, rhetorical questions, and other figures of speech.

## Definition
Irony is a figure of speech in which the sense that the speaker intends to communicate is actually the **opposite** of the literal meaning of the words. The speaker does not believe what they are literally saying. People use irony to:
- Emphasize how different something is from what it should be
- Show that someone else's belief is wrong or foolish
- Rebuke, mock, or create humor

Key characteristics:
- The intended meaning is the **opposite** of the literal words
- The speaker does NOT believe the literal statement
- Often used for rebuke, mockery, or emphasis
- Sarcasm is a form of irony

## NOT figs-irony (Use These Instead)

### Use figs-hyperbole for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "Everyone is looking for you" | Exaggeration in same direction (many, not opposite) |
| "as numerous as sand" | Overstatement, not opposite meaning |
| "all wisdom of the Egyptians" | Means "very much," not the opposite |

**Key distinction**: Hyperbole exaggerates in the **same direction** (says "all" meaning "very many"). Irony says the **opposite** (says "good" meaning "bad").

### Use figs-rquestion for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Questions expecting agreement | Rhetorical purpose, not opposite meaning |
| Questions expressing emotion | Unless the content itself is ironic |

**Note**: A rhetorical question CAN contain irony (e.g., "Are you saying to kill me?" - sarcastic question). Tag both if applicable.

### Use figs-metaphor for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Comparisons where X is spoken of as Y | Active comparison, not opposite meaning |

## Irony Categories with Examples

### 1. Mocking with False Titles/Claims
The speaker uses a title or claim they do not believe to mock.

- **"King of the Jews"** - soldiers mocking Jesus (Matt 27:37, Mark 15:26, John 19:19)
- **"Christ"** - those hitting Jesus did not believe it (Matt 26:68)
- **"for he is a god"** - Elijah mocking Baal's prophets (1 Kings 18:27)
- **"He saved others"** - leaders mocking Jesus (Luke 23:35)
- **"but they are not [Jews]"** - acting contrary to what Jews should (Rev 2:9)

### 2. Praise for Wrong Actions (Rebuke)
Praising someone for something obviously wrong to rebuke them.

- **"How well you reject the commandment of God"** - Jesus to Pharisees (Mark 7:9)
- **"Already you are satisfied! Already you have become rich!"** - Paul to Corinthians (1 Cor 4:8)
- **"if indeed you have heard about him"** - Paul knows they have heard; rebuking inconsistency (Eph 4:21)

### 3. Using Others' False Self-Claims
Describing people with claims they make about themselves that the speaker does not believe.

- **"the wise and intelligent"** - those who think they are (Matt 11:25, Luke 10:21)
- **"the righteous"** - those who wrongly believe themselves righteous (Luke 5:32)
- **"the foolishness of God"** - using critics' perspective (1 Cor 1:21, 25)
- **"the weakness of God"** - speaking from world's perspective (1 Cor 1:25)

### 4. Absurd/Impossible Suggestions
Stating something impossible to mock or express frustration.

- **"Save yourself and us"** - criminal mocking Jesus (Luke 23:39)
- **"unless we go buy food for all these people"** - disciples' sarcasm (Luke 9:13)
- **"For which of those works are you stoning me?"** - Jesus knows it's not for good works (John 10:32)

### 5. Stating Obvious Falsehoods for Emphasis
Saying something obviously false to emphasize the opposite.

- **"You know, for you were born then"** - Job was NOT there at creation (Job 38:21)
- **"the joy of his way"** - godless person actually experiences sorrow (Job 8:19)
- **"you may mock"** - Job saying they should NOT mock (Job 21:3)
- **"Teach us what we shall say"** - Elihu does not believe they can (Job 37:19)

### 6. False Humility / Self-Description
Ironically describing oneself using opponents' characterizations.

- **"who am meek when face to face...but bold when absent"** - Paul quoting critics (2 Cor 10:1)
- **"Glorify yourself over me"** - Moses' ironic false humility (Exod 8:9)

### 7. Polite Irony (Stating While Denying)
Saying something while claiming not to say it.

- **"in order not to say to you"** - Paul says it while saying he won't (Phlm 1:19)
- **"I do not wish to speak to you"** - while speaking

### 8. Sarcastic Questions
Questions that are ironic in their content.

- **"Are you saying to kill me like you killed the Egyptian?"** - sarcastic threat (Exod 2:14)
- **"you say"** - disciples showing exasperation (Mark 5:31)

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify potential irony**: Does the speaker seem to mean the opposite of what they're literally saying?

2. **Apply the irony test**:
   - Does the speaker NOT believe the literal statement?
   - Is the intended meaning the OPPOSITE of the words?
   - Is there rebuke, mockery, emphasis, or humor involved?

3. **Distinguish from similar figures**:

   | Test | If YES | If NO |
   |------|--------|-------|
   | Speaker means OPPOSITE of literal words? | Likely irony | Check other figures |
   | Speaker exaggerating in SAME direction? | Hyperbole | Could be irony |
   | Using someone else's false claim about themselves? | Likely irony | Check context |
   | Mocking with title speaker doesn't believe? | Likely irony | Check context |

4. **Key distinction: irony vs hyperbole**:
   - **Irony**: Says the OPPOSITE of what is meant ("How well you disobey" = "You disobey terribly")
   - **Hyperbole**: Exaggerates in the SAME direction ("Everyone is looking" = "Many are looking")

5. **Check for combined figures**:
   - Rhetorical questions can contain ironic content - tag both
   - Ironic statements can use metaphorical language

---

## Critical Distinctions

### Irony vs Hyperbole:
- "How well you reject God's command" = IRONY (praise for bad thing = criticism)
- "Everyone is looking for you" = HYPERBOLE (exaggeration, not opposite)

### Irony vs Rhetorical Question:
- Both can occur together
- "Are you saying to kill me?" = figs-irony (sarcastic) AND figs-rquestion (not expecting answer)
- Pure rhetorical question without ironic content = figs-rquestion only

### Ironic Titles vs Genuine Titles:
- "Christ" by mockers = figs-irony (they don't believe it)
- "Christ" by believers = not ironic

### False Claims About Others:
- "the wise" (people who think they are) = figs-irony
- "the wise" (genuinely wise people) = not ironic

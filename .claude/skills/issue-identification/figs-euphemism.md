# figs-euphemism

## Purpose
Identify euphemisms in biblical text - mild or polite expressions used to refer to something unpleasant, embarrassing, or socially unacceptable.

## Definition
A euphemism is a mild or polite way of referring to something that is unpleasant, embarrassing, or socially unacceptable, such as death, sexual activity, or bodily functions.

Key characteristics:
- Uses indirect language to avoid offense or embarrassment
- The author/speaker intentionally avoids stating something directly
- Common across cultures but specific expressions vary by culture
- Purpose is politeness or propriety, NOT comparison or association

## NOT figs-euphemism (Use These Instead)

### Use figs-metonymy for Death/Battle Language:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "fallen" (died in battle) | Association - soldiers fall when mortally wounded |
| "by the sword" (in battle) | Association - sword represents battle |
| "blood on your head" | Association - blood represents death/guilt |

### Use figs-metonymy for Concrete Imagery Representing Abstract Concepts:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "nakedness" (= humiliation/defeat) | Association - exposure represents vulnerability |
| "darkness" (= evil/ignorance) | Association - darkness is associated with evil |

**Test:** Is the speaker being polite/discreet about an embarrassing topic? If no — if a concrete image simply represents an associated abstract concept — use metonymy, not euphemism.

Contrast with actual euphemisms for death:
- "lies down with his fathers" = euphemism (polite way to say died)
- "fallen asleep" = euphemism (polite way to say died)
- "fallen" (in battle) = metonymy (association with how soldiers die)

### Use figs-idiom for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "in the eyes of" | Fixed cultural phrase, not about politeness |
| "from the face of" (causal) | Set phrase meaning "because of" |
| "nose burns" (anger) | Cultural idiom for emotion |
| "struck to the mouth of the sword" | Set phrase for killing |
| "son of X" (characteristic) | Set phrase for type of person |

### Use figs-metaphor for:

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify potential euphemism**: Is the author/speaker avoiding direct statement of something?

2. **Check the topic**: Is it about something that could be:
   - Unpleasant (death, violence, punishment)
   - Embarrassing (bodily functions, conditions)
   - Socially sensitive (sexual activity)
   - Religiously sensitive (God's name, cursing God)

3. **Apply the euphemism test**:
   - Is the purpose to be polite or avoid offense?
   - Would stating this directly be uncomfortable?
   - Is the expression culturally understood to mean something else?
   - Is it NOT primarily a comparison (metaphor) or association (metonymy)?

4. **Distinguish from similar figures**:

   | Test | If YES | If NO |
   |------|--------|-------|
   | Avoiding unpleasant/embarrassing topic? | Likely euphemism | Check other figures |
   | Active comparison (X as if Y)? | Metaphor | Could be euphemism |
   | Association (X represents related Y)? | Metonymy | Could be euphemism |
   | Set phrase with fixed meaning? | Could be idiom | Could be euphemism |
   | Polite address to superior? | writing-politeness | Could be euphemism |

---

## Key Distinctions

### Euphemism vs. Idiom
- **Euphemism**: Purpose is to avoid offense/embarrassment about the TOPIC
- **Idiom**: Set phrase with fixed meaning, topic may not be sensitive

Example: "fell asleep" (= died) is euphemism - death is the sensitive topic being softened
Example: "in the eyes of" (= in judgment of) is idiom - not avoiding sensitive topic

### Euphemism vs. Metaphor
- **Euphemism**: Indirect reference to avoid discomfort
- **Metaphor**: Active comparison for imagery/emphasis

Example: "knew his wife" is euphemism - avoiding direct mention of sex
Example: "heart of stone" is metaphor - comparing stubbornness to stone

### Sexual Euphemisms - Context Matters
The same expression may be translated differently based on context:
- "knew" for marital relations: translate politely
- "knew" for assault/sin: may need clearer translation

## Common Euphemism Patterns by Testament

### Old Testament (Hebrew)
- Sexual: yadah ("knew"), bo el ("went to"), shakab ("lay with")
- Death: shakab im avot ("lay with fathers"), asaph el ("gathered to")
- Bodily: kasah raglayim ("cover feet")

### New Testament (Greek)
- Sexual: ginosko ("knew"), synerchomai ("came together")
- Death: koimaomai ("fallen asleep"), ekpneo ("expired")
- Religious: ouranos for theos ("heaven" for "God")

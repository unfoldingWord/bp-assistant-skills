# translate-kinship

## Purpose
Identify when kinship terms require notes to help translators choose appropriate relationship terminology in their language.

## Definition
Kinship terms describe familial relationships. Languages vary significantly in how specific these terms are - some distinguish age, gender of speaker, side of family, or marital status. Biblical text may use terms that are more or less specific than the target language requires.

## When to Write Notes

| Category | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| In-law relationships | Languages distinguish perspective (wife's father vs. husband's father) | "Jethro, father-in-law of Moses" |
| Sibling age/order | Many languages require older vs. younger distinction | "his brothers" (1 Sam 16:13) - David's older brothers |
| Full vs. half siblings | Some languages distinguish same mother/father | Jesus' "brothers and sisters" - half-siblings |
| Extended family | Clans, ancestors, descendants, general relatives | "house of your father," "from the clan of Elimelech" |
| Affectionate/figurative | Kinship terms for non-relatives | "my daughter" (Boaz to Ruth) - showing affection |
| Kinsman-redeemer | Hebrew technical term for closest male relative with obligations | Legal term requiring explanation |

## NOT This Issue

| Use Instead | When |
|-------------|------|
| translate-names | Person introduced by name and relationship; focus is on identifying the person |
| figs-metaphor | "fathers" = ancestors, "sons" = descendants, "house of X" = descendants, "brothers" = fellow Israelites/believers |
| figs-gendernotations | "brothers" includes women (generic masculine), "sons of Israel" includes daughters |
| writing-participants | Introducing a new character where kinship term is incidental |

## Recognition Triggers

Look for translate-kinship when:
1. Term describes relationship between specific people
2. Target language likely has more specific terms available
3. Context provides information about age, side of family, or other distinctions
4. Relationship term might need explanation for the target culture
5. Text uses a general term where specific term exists in many languages

**Key terms:** brother/sister (age), father/mother (in-law forms), son/daughter (in-law forms), uncle/nephew/cousin, clan/extended family, kinsman-redeemer

## Categories

### Required Notes (high priority)
- In-law relationships (specific side matters)
- Sibling order when context reveals it
- Kinsman-redeemer (technical term)
- Figurative uses of kinship terms for non-relatives

### Optional Notes (context-dependent)
- Generic "brother/sister" without age indication
- Clan/extended family references
- Ancestor/descendant language (unless better as figs-metaphor)

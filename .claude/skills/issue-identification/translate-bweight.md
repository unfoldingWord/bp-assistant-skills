# translate-bweight

## Purpose
Identify biblical weight measurements that may need translation notes.

## Definition
Biblical weights were used to measure both objects and precious metals (which functioned as money before coinage). The shekel was the basic unit; other weights were multiples or fractions of it.

## Common Weight Units

| Unit | Shekels | Grams | Kilograms |
|------|---------|-------|-----------|
| gerah | 1/20 | 0.57 g | - |
| bekah | 1/2 | 5.7 g | - |
| pim | 2/3 | 7.6 g | - |
| shekel | 1 | 11 g | - |
| mina | 50 | 550 g | ~0.5 kg |
| talent/kikkar | 3,000 | - | 34 kg |

## When to Write Notes

Write notes when these weight terms appear:
- **shekel(s)** - Basic unit of weight
- **talent(s)** / **kikkar(s)** - Large weight (~34 kg)
- **mina(s)** - Medium weight (~0.5 kg)
- **gerah** - Small weight (~0.57 g)
- **bekah** - Half shekel
- **pim** - Two-thirds shekel

## NOT translate-bweight

| Use This Instead | When |
|------------------|------|
| translate-bmoney | NT coins (denarii, drachma) or clear currency context |
| translate-bdistance | Cubits, spans (distance) |
| translate-bvolume | Ephah, hin (volume) |

## Key Distinction: Weight vs. Currency

**Use translate-bweight** when measuring:
- Physical objects: "the spear weighed 300 shekels"
- Metal by weight: "weighed out 400 shekels of silver"
- Precious materials for construction

**Use translate-bmoney** when:
- NT coins are named (denarii, drachma, mite)
- Clear payment/wages context in NT

**OT often overlaps**: "paid X shekels of silver" is both weight AND payment. Use translate-bweight for OT metal transactions.

## Translation Principles (from TA)

1. Original measures show ancient context
2. Modern measures help readers understand
3. If possible, mention both in text or footnote
4. Avoid false precision (say "about half a kilogram" not "550 grams")
5. Use "about" for general measurements
6. Do NOT use "about" when God specifies exact weights for offerings

## Recognition Patterns

Look for:
- "X shekels of [gold/silver/bronze/iron]"
- "X talents" - large weight amounts
- "mina(s)" / "minas" - medium amounts
- Weights of weapons, armor, construction materials

## Contextual Clues for Weight vs. Money

**Weight context**:
- Describing objects (spears, crowns, chains)
- Construction materials (gold overlay, bronze fittings)
- "weighed out" language

**Money context**:
- Price/payment transactions
- Wages, debts, fines
- Combined with "pieces" or "coins"

## Note Approaches

### Option 1: Keep Biblical Term
Transliterate ("shekel," "talent") - shows ancient context

### Option 2: Use Modern Equivalent
Convert to grams/kilograms - helps readers understand

### Option 3: Provide Both
"600 shekels (about 7 kilograms)" - gives both advantages

## Examples from Published Notes

**Exodus 38:29**: "70 talents and 2,400 shekels"
- Notes explain: ~2,400 kilograms total

**1 Kings 10:16**: "600 gold shekels"
- Notes explain weight for each shield

**1 Kings 9:14**: "120 kikkars (talents)"
- Notes provide metric equivalent

**Exodus 25:39**: "from a talent of pure gold"
- Notes explain: ~34 kg

## Categories

### Small Weights (personal items)
- gerah, bekah, pim, shekel
- Jewelry, small tools, personal items

### Medium Weights
- Multiple shekels, minas
- Weapons, armor, household items

### Large Weights (bulk materials)
- Talents/kikkars
- Construction materials, tributes, treasures

### Sanctuary/Temple Standards
Some texts specify "sanctuary shekel" or "shekel of the sanctuary" - a standard weight used for sacred purposes.

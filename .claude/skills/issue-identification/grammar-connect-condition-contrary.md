# grammar-connect-condition-contrary

## Purpose
Identify counterfactual conditions where the speaker uses a known-false premise to make a logical argument.

## Definition
A contrary-to-fact condition is a conditional statement where:
- The speaker is CERTAIN the condition is NOT true
- The speaker uses the false premise to make a LOGICAL ARGUMENT
- The pattern is: "if A then B; but A is untrue; therefore, not B"

From Issues Resolved (Aug 6, 2025):
> grammar-connect-condition-contrary: counterfactual conditions ("if A, then B; but A is untrue; therefore, not B")

From Translation Academy:
> A Contrary-to-Fact Condition is a condition that sounds hypothetical, but the speaker is already certain that it is NOT true.

---

## CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Four Condition Types

| Issue | Use For | Key Test |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **grammar-connect-condition-contrary** | Counterfactual LOGICAL ARGUMENTS | Speaker KNOWS condition is false, uses it to prove something |
| **grammar-connect-condition-fact** | "If" meaning "since" | Condition is actually TRUE |
| **grammar-connect-condition-hypothetical** | Simple conditions needing "if/then" clarification | REAL possible condition, grammar unclear |
| **figs-hypo** | Imaginary scenarios for teaching | UNREAL scenario, not a logical argument |

### grammar-connect-condition-contrary vs figs-hypo

Both involve unreal conditions, but they serve different purposes:

| Feature | grammar-connect-condition-contrary | figs-hypo |
|---------|-----------------------------------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | PROVE something through logic | TEACH/ILLUSTRATE through imagination |
| **Structure** | "if A then B; A is false; so not B" | "Imagine X. What would happen?" |
| **Speaker's goal** | Demonstrate a logical conclusion | Help audience understand a principle |
| **Reality status** | Speaker KNOWS condition is false | Condition may be impossible, absurd, or just imaginary |

**Examples of figs-hypo (NOT condition-contrary):**
- 1 Cor 12:15-21: Body parts talking ("If the foot would say...") - ABSURD TEACHING SCENARIO
- Matt 7:9-10: "What man, if his son asks for bread..." - IMAGINARY ILLUSTRATION
- Matt 18:12: "If a man has 100 sheep and one goes astray..." - PARABLE SETUP
- Matt 21:25-26: "If we say X, he will say Y..." - DELIBERATION/PLANNING

**Examples of condition-contrary (NOT figs-hypo):**
- John 8:42: "If God were your Father, you would love me" - LOGICAL PROOF (they don't love, so God isn't their father)
- 1 Cor 15:13-19: "If there is no resurrection..." - REDUCTIO AD ABSURDUM
- Matt 12:26: "If Satan casts out Satan..." - LOGICAL ARGUMENT showing this isn't the case

**Quick Test**:
- Is the speaker PROVING something by showing what logically follows from a false premise? --> grammar-connect-condition-contrary
- Is the speaker TEACHING by creating an imaginary scenario to illustrate a point? --> figs-hypo

## Categories of grammar-connect-condition-contrary

### 1. Past Counterfactual ("if X had happened, Y would have happened")
The speaker knows X did NOT happen.

**Pattern**: "if [past perfect], [would have + past participle]"

| Reference | Text | Logic |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Matt 12:7 | "if you had known... you would not have condemned" | They condemned --> proves they didn't know |
| John 11:21 | "if you had been here, my brother would not have died" | Jesus was NOT there, brother DID die |
| 1 Cor 2:8 | "if they had understood, they would not have crucified" | They crucified --> proves they didn't understand |
| John 15:22 | "If I had not come and spoken... they would have no sin" | Jesus DID come --> they DO have sin |

### 2. Present Counterfactual ("if X were true, Y would be true")
The speaker knows X is NOT true right now.

**Pattern**: "if [past tense/subjunctive], [would + verb]"

| Reference | Text | Logic |
|-----------|------|-------|
| John 8:42 | "If God were your Father, you would love me" | They don't love him --> God is NOT their Father |
| John 18:36 | "If my kingdom were from this world, my servants would fight" | Servants NOT fighting --> kingdom NOT from this world |
| Luke 7:39 | "If this man were a prophet, he would know..." | Pharisee concludes Jesus must NOT be a prophet |
| Matt 12:26 | "if Satan casts out Satan, he has been divided" | Satan is NOT divided --> he does NOT cast out Satan |

### 3. "As if" / "As though" Constructions
Speaker acknowledges something is NOT the case but speaks as if it were.

| Reference | Text | Reality |
|-----------|------|---------|
| 1 Cor 5:3 | "as if being present" | Paul is NOT present but judgment is effective |
| 1 Cor 4:18 | "as though I were not coming" | Paul IS coming |

### 4. "Even if" with "So-called" or Explicit False-Marking
Speaker uses "even if" + explicit markers to show the condition is FALSE.

**Key markers**: "so-called", "supposed", "like"

| Reference | Text | False-Marking |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| 1 Cor 8:5 | "even if there are so-called gods... yet for us there is one God" | "so-called" marks as NOT real gods |
| 1 Cor 4:15 | "if you would have myriads of guardians in Christ" | Paul KNOWS they don't have myriads |
| 1 Cor 8:5 | "even if there are many gods and many lords" | Paul does NOT believe there are many gods |

### 5. "Otherwise" Introducing Contrary Result
Shows what would be true IF the previous statement were false.

| Reference | Text | Logic |
|-----------|------|-------|
| 1 Cor 7:14 | "Otherwise your children are unclean" | Paul does NOT think children are unclean; proves spouse IS sanctified |

### 6. Hypothetical Argument Chains (Reductio ad Absurdum)
Extended arguments showing absurd results from a false premise.

| Reference | Chain | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| 1 Cor 15:13-19 | "if there is no resurrection" --> "Christ has not been raised" --> "our preaching is vain" --> "your faith is worthless" | Paul KNOWS resurrection is real; shows absurd implications of denying it |

### 7. Impossibility Statements
Conditions that are impossible, used to make a comparison.

| Reference | Text | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Gen 13:16 | "if anyone were able to count [the dust]" | Impossible; emphasizes countless descendants |
| Gen 15:5 | "if you are able to count [the stars]" | Impossible; emphasizes promise |

## NOT grammar-connect-condition-contrary

### Use figs-hypo for:
| Expression | Why NOT contrary |
|------------|------------------|
| "If only we had died" (Exodus 16:3) | Expressing WISH/REGRET, not logical argument |
| "If the foot would say..." (1 Cor 12:15) | ABSURD teaching scenario, not proving something false |
| "Suppose someone said to God..." | IMAGINARY scenario for teaching |
| Body parts talking to each other | Impossible scenario for illustration |

### Use grammar-connect-condition-fact for:
| Expression | Why NOT contrary |
|------------|------------------|
| "If Yahweh is God, worship him" | Condition is TRUE (Elijah knows Yahweh IS God) |
| "If I am a father, where is my honor?" | Condition is TRUE (Yahweh IS a father) |

### Use grammar-connect-condition-hypothetical for:
| Expression | Why NOT contrary |
|------------|------------------|
| "A man, when he has..." | REAL possible condition, needs "if" |
| Missing "if" or "then" | Grammar issue, not counterfactual |

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify the condition**: Is there an "if...then" structure (explicit or implicit)?

2. **Check the speaker's certainty**: Does the speaker KNOW the condition is NOT true?
   - If uncertain whether true/false --> NOT contrary-to-fact
   - If speaker knows it's TRUE --> grammar-connect-condition-fact
   - If speaker knows it's FALSE --> Continue to step 3

3. **Check for logical argument structure**:
   - Is the speaker proving something by showing what would follow?
   - Is there a "if A then B; but A is false; so not B" pattern?
   - Is the result absurd/unacceptable, proving the premise false?
   - If YES --> grammar-connect-condition-contrary

4. **If no logical argument**:
   - Is it a wish/regret? --> figs-hypo
   - Is it an imaginary teaching scenario? --> figs-hypo

## Linguistic Markers

### English Markers for Contrary-to-Fact
- Past tense in "if" clause with present meaning
- "would have" + past participle (past counterfactual)
- "would" + verb (present counterfactual)
- Subjunctive "were" (instead of "was")
- "as if" / "as though"
- "even if" (when known false)
- "so-called", "supposed", "like" (explicit false-marking)
- "yet", "but", "however" (contrasting with truth)
- "Otherwise"

### Greek Markers
- Second-class conditional: ei + indicative... an + indicative (contrary-to-fact)
- Past tense forms indicating unreality

### Hebrew Markers
- lu (if only) for wishes/regrets (may overlap with figs-hypo)
- Counterfactual constructions with perfect + imperfect

## Summary: The Key Test

**grammar-connect-condition-contrary** = Speaker KNOWS condition is FALSE and uses it for LOGICAL ARGUMENT

Ask yourself:
1. Does the speaker know the "if" part is NOT true?
2. Is the speaker making a logical point by showing what would follow?
3. Does the argument have the form "if A then B; A is false; therefore not B"?

If YES to all three --> grammar-connect-condition-contrary

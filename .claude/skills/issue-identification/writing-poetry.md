# writing-poetry

## Purpose
Identify poetic constructions that may need explanation for translators, primarily the cognate accusative (verb + noun from same root).

## Definition
Poetry uses special language structures for beauty, emphasis, and memorability. The primary use of writing-poetry in translation notes is for the **cognate accusative** construction where a verb and its object share the same root. This also covers other poetic features like sound play and word patterns.

---

## The Primary Use: Cognate Accusative

**From Issues Resolved**: "The template that we're using for cognate accusative (verb and object from same root) is writing-poetry, because it's a topic that is explicitly mentioned in there. Ex: 'he dreamed a dream,' 'any sin that he sins,' 'he shouted a great shout,' 'sacrificing sacrifices'"

### Recognition
Look for verb + noun where:
- The English verb and noun are clearly related (sacrifice/sacrifices)
- The noun is the direct object of the verb
- Both come from the same Hebrew/Greek root

---

## Key Distinction: writing-poetry vs figs-reduplication

| writing-poetry (Cognate Accusative) | figs-reduplication |
|-------------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Verb + noun** from same root | **Same verb repeated** twice |
| "he dreamed a dream" | "dying, he shall die" |
| "sacrificing sacrifices" | "knowing, you know" |
| Verb-Object relationship | Verbal emphasis (infinitive absolute) |
| Use writing-poetry | Use figs-reduplication |

**Key**: If the repeated element is a NOUN (object of the verb), use writing-poetry. If both are VERB FORMS, use figs-reduplication.

## Secondary Use: Similar Sounds (Paronomasia)

Hebrew poets sometimes use words that sound alike for effect. This is difficult to reproduce in translation.

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Judg 15:16 "jawbone of a donkey, a heap, two heaps" | Hebrew chamor (donkey) sounds like chomer (heap) |
| Judg 5:12 "speak a song" | Hebrew d-b-r matches Deborah's name |
| Amos 8:1-2 "summer fruit" / "end" | Hebrew qayits sounds like qets |

### Recognition
Look for:
- ULT note indicating Hebrew wordplay
- Words that seem oddly repeated or paired
- Commentary noting sound similarities in Hebrew

## Other Poetry Markers

### General Poetry Identification
Use the generic template when text is poetic but no specific device needs explanation:
- Song of Songs 1:1 - "The Song of Songs"
- Jonah 2:2 - "I cried out to Yahweh from my distress" (beginning of poetic prayer)

### Multiple Synonyms
Poetry sometimes uses multiple words for the same concept for emphasis:
- Deut 4:1 - "statutes and rules and commandments" (multiple terms for God's instructions)

### Chiasm
Reversal of order creates poetic structure:
- Gen 2:4 - "made the earth and the heavens" (reverses "heavens and earth" from 2:1)

---

## NOT writing-poetry

### figs-reduplication
Same verb form repeated:
- "dying, you shall die" -> figs-reduplication (infinitive absolute)
- "near, near" -> figs-reduplication (word repetition)

### figs-parallelism
Two different clauses saying the same thing:
- "Praise Yahweh; exalt his name" -> figs-parallelism (different words/phrases)

### figs-doublet
Two different synonymous words:
- "scattered and dispersed" -> figs-doublet
- "statutes and rules" -> figs-doublet (when just two words, not verb+object pattern)

## Recognition Process

1. **Is there a verb + noun from the same root?**
   - "sacrificed sacrifices," "vowed a vow," "prayed a prayer"
   - -> writing-poetry (cognate accusative)

2. **Is there sound play between different words?**
   - Donkey/heap wordplay in Judges
   - -> writing-poetry (similar sounds)

3. **Is text explicitly identified as poetry/song?**
   - Title or introduction marks it as song/poem
   - -> writing-poetry (generic, if note needed)

4. **Is this repeated verb forms (not verb + noun)?**
   - "dying, he died" - infinitive absolute
   - -> Use figs-reduplication instead

## Genre Distribution

### Levitical/Sacrificial Texts
Most common: offering/present, sacrifice/sacrificing patterns
- Extensive in Leviticus (68 occurrences in published notes)
- Ritual language favors cognate accusative

### Historical Narrative (Samuel, Kings)
Common: vow/vowed, strike/striking, sacrifice/sacrificed, counsel/counseled
- Often in solemn or emphatic contexts

### Poetry Books (Psalms, Job, Proverbs)
Less common for cognate accusative; more often has other poetic markers

### Prophecy
Occasional cognate accusative; sound play in Hebrew prophets

### NT (Revelation)
Greek occasionally uses cognate accusative:
- Rev 17:6 "wondered a wonder"
- Rev 14:2 "harpists harping on harps"

## Issues Resolved Decisions

From content team meetings:
- **Cognate accusative goes to writing-poetry** (not figs-reduplication)
- **Poetry verb tenses**: "Since the aspect varies in poetry, we do not need to represent perfects with past tense and then write translate-tense notes. We can use the appropriate English tense."
- **Poetic images**: "Sometimes an image seems to have more than one possible meaning... In poetry, the author may be using an evocative image whose meaning he expects to be multivalent."

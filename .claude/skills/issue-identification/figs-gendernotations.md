# figs-gendernotations

## Purpose
Identify when masculine terms in Hebrew/Greek are used generically to include both men and women, but readers might not realize this.

## Definition
Biblical languages often use masculine words (man, men, brother, brothers, son, sons, he, him, his) in a generic sense that includes both men and women. English readers may misunderstand these as referring only to males.

## When to Write Notes

Write figs-gendernotations notes when:
1. A masculine term is used **generically** to include women
2. Readers **might not realize** the term includes women
3. The context does not make the inclusive meaning obvious

### Common Terms That Trigger This Issue

**Greek:**
- adelphoi (brothers) - often means "brothers and sisters" or "fellow believers"
- anthropos/anthropoi (man/men) - often means "person/people"
- aner/andres (man/men) - can mean "person/people" in generic contexts
- huios/huioi (son/sons) - "sons of God" often means "children of God"
- autos (he/him/his) - generic third person in proverbs/general statements

**Hebrew:**
- ben/bene (son/sons) - "my son" in Proverbs includes daughters; "sons of Israel" = "people of Israel"
- ish/anashim (man/men) - often "person/people"
- adam (man) - often "human/humanity"
- enosh (man) - often "mortal/person"
- geber (man) - often "person" in wisdom literature
- av/avot (father/fathers) - "your fathers" often means "ancestors"
- ach/achim (brother/brothers) - can include sisters in family contexts

## NOT This Issue

Do NOT use figs-gendernotations for:

1. **Terms already inclusive in English** - Words like "person" that are feminine in Hebrew but already read as inclusive in English need no note

2. **Male-specific contexts** - When the term genuinely refers only to males:
   - Levirate marriage laws (brother must marry his brother's wife)
   - Circumcision instructions
   - Priestly duties (historically male-only)
   - Contexts specifying "men and women" separately

3. **Proper names with gender** - Use translate-names instead when gender matters for understanding (e.g., "Zeruiah is the name of a woman")

4. **Frequent phrases in a book** - When a phrase like "sons of Israel" occurs repeatedly, discuss it in the book/chapter introduction rather than writing notes to every instance

## Confirmed Decisions (Issues Resolved)

- Write notes when **masculine terms include women but readers might not realize this**
- For **frequent phrases** (e.g., "the sons of Israel"), discuss in introduction; do not write notes to every instance
- **Not necessary** to write notes to words like "person" that are feminine in Hebrew but already inclusive in English
- Singular generic phrases like "a man who..." belong in figs-genericnoun instead of figs-gendernotations
- For gentilics (Jezreelitess, Shunammitess), ULT uses gendered forms matching Hebrew; this is separate from figs-gendernotations

## Categories

### 1. Kinship Terms
- **brothers** (adelphoi) -> "brothers and sisters" or "fellow believers"
- **sons** (huioi, bene) -> "children" or "descendants"
- **fathers** (pateres, avot) -> "ancestors"
- **my son** (beni) in Proverbs -> includes daughters as audience

### 2. Generic "Man" Terms
- **man** (anthropos, adam, ish, enosh, geber) -> "person" or "human"
- **men** (anthropoi, anashim) -> "people" or "humans"
- **mankind/humanity** references
- Singular generic phrases like "a man who..." belong in figs-genericnoun instead

### 3. Third-Person Pronouns
- Generic **he/him/his** in proverbs, laws, general statements
- "If anyone... he should..." constructions
- Wisdom literature maxims about "a man"

### 4. Collective/Addressee Terms
- **sons of Israel** -> "people of Israel" (but put in intro if frequent)
- **young man** (naar) in Proverbs -> includes young women

## Recognition Process

```
Is there a masculine term?
  |
  +-- NO --> Not this issue
  |
  +-- YES --> Is the term used generically (includes women)?
              |
              +-- NO (male-specific context) --> Not this issue
              |
              +-- YES --> Might readers miss the inclusive meaning?
                          |
                          +-- NO (obvious from context) --> Skip note
                          |
                          +-- YES --> Does this phrase occur frequently in the book?
                                      |
                                      +-- YES --> Discuss in introduction
                                      |
                                      +-- NO --> Write figs-gendernotations note
```

## Note Patterns from Examples

### Standard Pattern
"Although the term **[term]** is masculine, [SPEAKER] is using the word in a generic sense that includes both men and women. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use a phrase that makes this clear. Alternate translation: [inclusive term]"

### For "brothers"
"Although **brothers** is masculine, [SPEAKER] is using it to refer to both men and women. Alternate translation: [brothers and sisters]"

### For Pronouns
"Although the term **him** is masculine, [SPEAKER] is using the word in a generic sense that includes both men and women. Alternate translation: [him or her]"

### For Repeated Usage
"See how you translated the same use of this phrase in [reference]."

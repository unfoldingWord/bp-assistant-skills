# figs-genericnoun

## Purpose
Identify when singular nouns, pronouns, or noun phrases refer to categories or types in general rather than to specific individuals or things.

## Definition
Generic noun phrases use singular forms to represent an entire class or category. The speaker is not referring to one specific person, animal, object, or place, but to any member of that category. This is especially common in wisdom literature (Proverbs, Job) where general truths apply to anyone fitting the description.

## Recognition Markers

**Primary indicators:**
- Singular "the" + noun representing a class: "the righteous one" (= righteous people)
- Singular "a/an" + noun in proverbial context: "a man who..." (= anyone who)
- Singular people group names: "the Amorite" (= the Amorites)
- Singular animals as class representatives: "the ant," "the lion"
- Singular body parts with plural possessors: "their heart" (= their hearts)
- Participial phrases: "the one who gathers," "one who steals" (= anyone who)
- Generic pronouns: "he," "his" in general statements (= anyone, their)

**Context clues:**
- Proverbial or instructional statements
- Universal principles being stated
- Lists of clean/unclean animals or foods
- General laws or regulations
- Wisdom sayings about types of people

## Categories

### 1. People Groups (Singular for Plural)
Singular ethnic/tribal designations representing the whole group.
- "the Hittite, and the Amorite" --> "the Hittites, and the Amorites"
- "the Danite" --> "the Danites"
- "the Jew...the Greek" --> "Jewish people...Greek people"

### 2. Types of People (Wisdom Literature)
Singular persons representing anyone with that characteristic.
- "the righteous one" --> "any righteous person" / "righteous people"
- "the wicked one" --> "any wicked person"
- "a man who does X" --> "anyone who does X"
- "the fool" --> "foolish people"

### 3. Participial Generics
Verbal phrases describing anyone who does the action.
- "the one believing" --> "anyone who believes"
- "one who gathers" --> "any person who gathers"
- "the thief...he steals" --> "thieves...they steal"

### 4. Animals and Objects (Category Representatives)
Singular items representing all of that type.
- "the camel" --> "camels" / "any camel"
- "the ant" --> "ants"
- "the sword" --> "any sword" / "swords"
- "the field" --> "fields"

### 5. Body Parts with Plural Possessors
Singular body parts when referring to multiple people's bodies.
- "your mortal body" (plural audience) --> "your mortal bodies"
- "their heart" --> "their hearts"
- "the mouth speaks" --> "mouths speak"

### 6. Generic Pronouns
He/she/his/her referring to any person in that category.
- "he steals...his appetite" --> "they steal...their appetites"
- "a man...his feet" --> "any person...that person's feet"

## NOT This Issue

### Use grammar-collectivenouns instead when:
- A singular noun refers to a GROUP AS A UNIT: "Israel went" (the nation as entity)
- The verb treats plural as singular: "this people has turned"
- A group name functions as subject of singular verb

### Use figs-nominaladj instead when:
- An ADJECTIVE (not a noun) is used as a noun: "the poor" (adjective), "the wise"
- The form is clearly adjectival functioning nominally

### Use figs-gendernotations instead when:
- A masculine term generically includes women: "brothers" (= siblings)
- "Sons of Israel" when clearly including all descendants
- "Man" meaning "humanity" (not individual generic person)

## Key Distinctions

| This Issue | Not This Issue |
|------------|----------------|
| "the righteous **one**" (noun phrase) | "the righteous" (nominal adjective) |
| "the Hittite" (people group singular) | "Israel" as collective subject |
| "the ant" (category of creatures) | "the flock" (collective noun for group) |
| "a man" in general statement | "brothers" including women |

## Issues Resolved Guidance
- "The ULT is likely to be singular (figs-genericnoun) and say e.g. 'the Hittite, and the Amorite, the Canaanite and the Perizzite, the Hivite, and the Jebusite.'"
- The UST should use plurals: "the Hittites, the Amorites, the Canaanites, the Perizzites, the Hivites, and the Jebusites."

## Common Patterns by Genre

**Proverbs/Wisdom:** Heavy use for general truths about types of people
**Law (Leviticus):** Animals and objects as category representatives
**Narrative:** People group singulars, generic "a man" for unspecified individuals
**Epistles:** Generic "man," "woman," "husband," "wife" in teaching
**Prophets:** People group singulars, generic "the wicked," "the one who..."

## Alternate Translation Strategies
1. Use plural form: "the ant" --> "ants"
2. Add "any": "a man" --> "any man" / "anyone"
3. Add "every/each": "the one who" --> "everyone who"
4. Use indefinite expressions: "whoever," "those who"

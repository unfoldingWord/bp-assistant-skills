# figs-go

## Purpose
Identify when the biblical text uses motion verbs ("go/come", "bring/take") that may need alternative translations due to differences in how languages express perspective and direction.

## Definition
Different languages have different ways of determining whether to use "go" or "come" and "take" or "bring" when talking about motion. The choice depends on the perspective (viewpoint) taken in the narrative - where the speaker or narrator is conceptually positioned.

Key characteristics:
- Motion verbs are **deictic** - their meaning depends on perspective/viewpoint
- Hebrew (and English) may use "come" where another language would say "go"
- The choice affects which direction the reader understands people are moving
- This is NOT about figurative language - it's about literal motion from different viewpoints

---

## IMPORTANT: When to Write figs-go Notes

From Issues Resolved (FINAL AUTHORITY):

> "A figs-go note is **not needed for most uses of 'go.'** Usually the perspective of the speaker is clear. However, when the narrator is speaking and the perspective could be that of one character or another, a note may be helpful." (Sep 25, 2024)

> "Figs-go notes may address 'bring' vs. 'take' when motion/travel is in view. But in general, **we are sparing with figs-go notes**. We do not write them to every instance of 'go' or 'come.'" (Jul 9, 2024)

### Write notes ONLY when:
1. The narrator's perspective is unclear (could be from either character's viewpoint)
2. The motion verb choice might genuinely confuse readers about direction
3. The translation would differ based on which perspective is taken

### Do NOT write notes when:
1. The speaker's perspective is obvious from context
2. The direction of motion is already clear
3. The issue is about elevation (go up/go down) - discuss in book intro only

---

## NOT figs-go (Handle Differently)

### Elevation expressions ("go up/go down"):
> "We will not write notes to 'go up/go down' as an idiom, since this is just something that Hebrew marks that English doesn't mark. There can be a brief discussion in the book introduction, with no support reference and no individual note pointing back to the introduction at the first instance." (Jul 9, 2024)

| Expression | Handling |
|------------|----------|
| "went up to Jerusalem" | Book introduction discussion only - NOT individual notes |
| "came down from the mountain" | Book introduction discussion only - NOT individual notes |
| "went down to Egypt" | Book introduction discussion only - NOT individual notes |

### Clear perspective situations (no note needed):
| Situation | Why no note |
|-----------|-------------|
| Character speaking to someone present | Perspective is clearly the addressee |
| Narrator describing from one character's known location | Perspective is obvious |
| Return to a previously established location | Direction is clear from context |

## Categories of figs-go Issues

### 1. Come/Go Alternation
The most common category. The Hebrew verb can be translated as either "came" or "went" depending on the perspective taken.

**Write a note when perspective is ambiguous:**
- Narrator could be positioned with either character
- Reader might be confused about direction of movement

**Examples from published notes:**
- "And Bathsheba came" - could be "went" (1 Kings 1:15)
- "And they came" - could be "went" (1 Kings 9:28)
- "he came to Pharaoh" - could be "went" (Genesis 41:14)

### 2. Brought/Took Alternation
Same principle applied to causative motion verbs.

**Write a note when:**
- The direction of transport is unclear from context
- Both "brought" and "took" would make sense

**Examples from published notes:**
- "he brought them" - could be "took them" (Genesis 2:19)
- "he brought her to the man" - could be "took her" (Genesis 2:22)
- "So he brought the people down" - could be "took" (Judges 7:5)

### 3. Gone/Come Alternation (Past Tense)
Past tense versions of the same issue.

**Examples:**
- "he has gone" - could be "has come" (2 Corinthians 8:17)
- "Men have gone out" - could be "have come out" (1 Kings 20:17)

### 4. Came out/Went out Alternation
Motion outward from a location.

**Examples:**
- "Jael came out to meet him" - could be "went out" (Judges 4:22)
- "Then Noah went out" - could be "came out" (Genesis 8:18)

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify motion verb**: Is this a verb of motion (go/come, bring/take, arrive/depart)?

2. **Check if perspective is clear**:
   - Is the speaker's/narrator's viewpoint obvious?
   - Is there only one reasonable interpretation of direction?
   - If YES to either, **do not write a note**

3. **Check if it's an elevation issue**:
   - Is this "go up" or "go down" based on geography?
   - If YES, handle in book introduction, **not individual notes**

4. **Determine if note is warranted**:
   - Could the perspective reasonably be from either character?
   - Would a translator in another language need to make a different choice?
   - If YES to both, write a note

5. **Choose appropriate template**:
   - came -> went: Use "came" template
   - went -> came: Use "went" template
   - brought -> took: Use "brought" template

---

## Critical Distinctions

### Perspective determination:
| Clue | Likely Perspective |
|------|-------------------|
| Narrator focuses on location A, character moves toward A | Use "came" |
| Narrator focuses on location A, character moves away from A | Use "went" |
| Character is speaking to addressee at location B | Motion toward B = "come", away = "go" |
| Story is following a character's journey | Perspective moves with that character |

### When both work equally well:
If both "came" and "went" (or "brought" and "took") work equally well in the context, the note simply alerts translators that their language might prefer one over the other. The note does not insist one is correct.

## Examples by Genre

### Narrative (OT)
- Genesis 7:16: "And those that went in" - perspective could be inside or outside ark
- Genesis 8:18: "Then Noah went out" - perspective from inside or outside?
- 1 Samuel 10:14: "so we came to Samuel" - Saul's perspective or location-based?

### Narrative (NT)
- John 9:7: "came back" - from Jesus's location or the man's home?
- John 19:5: "went" - from Pilate's position or crowd's position?

### Epistles
- Galatians 1:17: "I went to" - from Paul's starting point
- Galatians 2:11: "came" - to Antioch where the readers know the story happened
- 2 John 1:12: "to come" - from John's or recipients' perspective?

## Common Patterns in Published Notes

### Standard note phrasing:
- "In a context such as this, your language might say 'went' instead of **came**."
- "Your language may say 'came' rather than **went** in contexts such as this. Use whichever is more natural."
- "Use the motion verb that is most natural in your language."

### Notes that explain context:
- "The perspective of the narrative is set at the well. The daughters came from elsewhere to the well."
- "The Hebrew verb can be translated as **went** or 'came,' depending on the perspective that is taken."

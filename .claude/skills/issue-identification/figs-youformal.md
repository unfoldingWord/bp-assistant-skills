# figs-youformal

## Purpose
Identify when translators need guidance choosing between formal and informal forms of "you" based on speaker relationships.

## Definition (from Translation Academy)
Some languages make a distinction between **formal** and **informal** forms of "you." The formal form is typically used when speaking to:
- Someone older
- Someone in authority
- Strangers or people not known well

The informal form is used with:
- Family members
- Close friends
- People younger or of equal/lesser status

**Important:** Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, and English do NOT have formal/informal distinctions for "you." This issue exists entirely to help translators whose languages DO make this distinction.

## When to Use figs-youformal

This is a **rarely used** issue (only 8 occurrences in published notes). Use it when:

1. **The relationship requires guidance** - Especially when addressing God
2. **Cultural expectations might differ** - What's respectful varies by culture
3. **The context is particularly significant** - Key prayers, formal addresses

## Confirmed Classifications

### USE figs-youformal when:

1. **Addressing God in prayer**
   - "Use your best judgment about whether the formal or informal form of **you** would be more natural in your language here"
   - Example: Jesus praying to the Father (Luke 10:21, 22:42)

2. **Addressing a superior respectfully**
   - "If your language has a formal form of **you** that it uses to address a superior respectfully, it would be appropriate to use that form here"

3. **Contexts where register is theologically significant**
   - Joel crying out to Yahweh (Joel 1:19-20)

### What the TA Article Emphasizes:

The article specifically notes complexity around addressing Jesus:
> "Use of 'you' by people speaking to Jesus is sometimes particularly difficult for translators. Because Jesus is God, some will want to always use the formal form when people are speaking to him, but it is important to recognize the actual relationship and feelings towards Jesus. Pharisees and Sadducees became Jesus' enemies early on and would have been unlikely to have spoken with particular respect to him."

## NOT This Issue (Use Instead)

| Situation | Use Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| Singular vs plural "you" | figs-yousingular |
| Exactly two people | figs-youdual |
| Singular to a crowd | figs-youcrowd |
| General "you" clarification | figs-you |
| Polite speech patterns | writing-politeness |

## Recognition Process

```
Is there a second-person pronoun addressing someone?
|
+--> Is the relationship one where formal/informal matters?
     |
     +--> God being addressed
     |    --> Consider figs-youformal
     |
     +--> Superior/authority being addressed
     |    --> Consider figs-youformal (rare)
     |
     +--> Peer or informal relationship
          --> Usually no note needed
          --> May need writing-politeness for polite speech
```

## Why This Issue Is Rare

1. **No biblical language has this distinction** - So it's never "in the text"
2. **Highly culture-dependent** - What's formal varies enormously
3. **Usually self-evident** - Translators know their own language's norms
4. **Only flagged for ambiguous cases** - Especially God/prayer

## Alternate Translations

Alternate translations are generally **not provided** for figs-youformal notes because:
- The choice depends entirely on the target language's conventions
- English cannot model formal/informal distinctions
- The note simply guides the translator to make the appropriate choice

## Key Theological Consideration

The TA article notes the cultural variability in addressing God:
> "Some cultures would use the formal 'you' because God is in authority. Other cultures would use the informal 'you' because God is our Father."

This means notes should not prescribe formal or informal but help translators understand the relationship so they can apply their own cultural norms.

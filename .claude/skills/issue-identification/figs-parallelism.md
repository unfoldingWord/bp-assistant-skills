# figs-parallelism

## Purpose
Identify synonymous parallelism in biblical text where two clauses express the same idea with different words for emphasis.

## Definition
Parallelism is a poetic device in which two **phrases or clauses** with similar structure or meaning are used together. The key type for translation notes is **synonymous parallelism**: where the second clause/phrase repeats the meaning of the first using different words.

From Issues Resolved: "A parallelism is two or more clauses (= subject + verb; subject may be implied in an imperative) with the same meaning used together for emphasis."

---

## Parallelism vs Doublet: The Key Distinction

| Parallelism | Doublet |
|-------------|---------|
| Two or more **clauses** (subject + verb) | Two **words or short phrases** |
| "Praise Yahweh, all nations; / exalt him, all peoples" | "scattered and dispersed" |
| Full parallel thoughts | Single intensified idea |
| Often spans poetic lines | Often joined with "and" |

**The boundary is fuzzy**: Long synonymous phrases may feel more like parallelism than doublet. Editorial discretion applies.

**Critical**: We do NOT write notes for **synthetic parallelism** (second clause extends/completes first). Only **synonymous parallelism** (second clause repeats first in different words).

**Note:** Parallel lines often involve ellipsis - the second line may omit verbs or other elements from the first. See [figs-ellipsis](figs-ellipsis.md). When identifying parallelism, also check for ellipsis in the second/third line. However, if the word that would be "supplied" already appears in the ULT within curly braces `{}`, it is not ellipsis -- ULT has already handled it.

## Types of Synonymous Parallelism

### Standard Parallel Clauses
Two clauses saying the same thing differently:
- "Yahweh sees everything a person does / and watches all the paths he takes" (Prov 5:21)
- "Praise Yahweh, all you nations; / exalt him, all you peoples" (Ps 117:1)
- "You make him to rule over the works of your hands; / you have put all things under his feet" (Ps 8:6)

### Positive-Negative Restatement
First states positively, second states negatively (or vice versa):
- "all of the army fell / Not even one remained"
- "he has turned from after me / and has not raised up my words"
- "Samuel declared all the words / and did not hide anything from him"

### Emphasis Through Repetition
The same verb or concept repeated for emphasis:
- "With extreme corruption we have acted corruptly" (Neh 1:7)
- "keep my commandments and do them" (Neh 1:9)
- "three against two and two against three" (Luke 12:52)

### Extreme Statements for Effect
Numerical intensification common in Hebrew poetry:
- "twice, thrice" = again and again (Job 33:29)

## Recognition Process

1. **Look for parallel structure**: Two clauses with similar grammatical patterns

2. **Check if meaning is synonymous**: Do both clauses express the same basic idea?

3. **Verify it's clause-level** (not just word pairs):
   - Contains subject + verb (subject may be implied)
   - If just two words/short phrases joined by "and" -> consider figs-doublet

4. **Poetic line test**: If synonymous expressions appear on different poetic lines (`\q1` / `\q2`), this strongly indicates parallelism rather than doublet, even if the parallel elements are short phrases. Poetic line breaks are a structural indicator of clause-level parallelism.

5. **Confirm it's synonymous** (not synthetic):
   - Second clause REPEATS meaning of first = synonymous (write note)
   - Second clause EXTENDS/COMPLETES first = synthetic (no note needed)

6. **Consider the effect**: What is the author emphasizing by saying it twice?

7. **For tricola** (3-stanza verses): Check if all three lines express the same idea. If so, write one parallelism note covering all three lines rather than separate notes.

## NOT figs-parallelism

### Synthetic Parallelism (no note needed)
The second clause adds new information or completes the thought:
- "Your word is a lamp to my feet / and a light for my path" - This IS synonymous parallelism (both describe guidance)
- "the king was passing by, and he cried out" - NOT parallelism, sequential events

### figs-doublet (use instead)
Just two words or very short phrases:
- "scattered and dispersed" -> figs-doublet
- "false and deceptive words" -> figs-doublet
- "without blemish and without spot" -> figs-doublet

### figs-hendiadys (use instead)
One word modifies the other (not equal synonyms):
- "glory and beauty" where one modifies -> figs-hendiadys

### Simple Repetition
Some repetition is for clarity or structure, not emphasis - context determines if a note is needed.

## Quote Selection

When creating the quote field for parallelism, include both (or all three) complete parallel lines. Do not narrow the quote to just the 1-2 words where the synonymy is most visible. The reader needs to see the full parallel structure to understand the note.

Good: "Praise Yahweh, all you nations; exalt him, all you peoples"
Bad: "Praise ... exalt" or just "all nations ... all peoples"

## AT Strategies from TA

For languages where synonymous parallelism is confusing:

1. **Combine into one clause**
   - "Until now you have deceived me with your lies" (combining "dealt deceitfully" + "spoken lies")

2. **Add emphasis word** ("truly," "certainly")
   - "You have certainly made him to rule over everything you created"

3. **Use intensifying language** ("very," "completely," "all")
   - "All you have done is lie to me"
   - "Yahweh sees absolutely everything that a person does"

From Issues Resolved: "For figs-parallelism, a variety of ATs can be suggested, beyond replacing 'A, and B' with 'A, yes, B.'"

## Common AT Patterns

| Source Pattern | AT Options |
|---------------|------------|
| "A and B" | "A, yes, B" / "A, that is, B" |
| Two clauses | Combine with emphasis word |
| Positive + negative | Express as strong positive |
| Numerical ("twice, thrice") | "again and again" |

## Genre Considerations

### Poetry (Psalms, Proverbs, prophets, Job)
- Parallelism is the foundation of Hebrew poetry
- Generally preserve both clauses when possible
- Use Hebrew poetry template to explain the device

### Narrative (Genesis, Samuel, etc.)
- Less common, used for special emphasis
- May combine clauses more freely
- Often signals something important

### NT Epistles and Gospels
- Appears in quotations from OT poetry
- Sometimes used by speakers for emphasis
- Greek parallelism functions similarly to Hebrew

## Key Decisions from Issues Resolved

- We do NOT write notes for synthetic parallelism, only for synonymous parallelism
- The boundary between doublet and parallelism is fuzzy - editorial discretion
- A variety of ATs can be suggested, beyond just "A, yes, B"
- In poetry, psalms pair terms like "rejoice" and "be glad" - ULT preserves both

# figs-distinguish

## Purpose
Identify when modifying phrases that provide information or reminders about a noun might be misunderstood as distinguishing that noun from similar items.

## Definition
In some languages, phrases that modify a noun can serve two different purposes:
1. **Distinguishing**: Identifying which specific noun is meant from among similar items
2. **Informing/Reminding**: Providing additional information or reminders about the noun

Some languages only use modifying phrases for distinguishing. When speakers of these languages hear a modifying phrase, they assume it distinguishes one item from another. This can cause confusion when the phrase is actually providing information or a reminder.

---

## CRITICAL: What figs-distinguish Is and Is NOT

### figs-distinguish IS FOR:
**Modifying phrases that provide information or reminders** but might be misread as distinguishing:
- "**Mordecai, the Jew**" - reminds who Mordecai is, NOT distinguishing from other Mordecais
- "**Noah, a preacher of righteousness**" - describes Noah, NOT distinguishing from another Noah
- "**your righteous judgments**" - all God's judgments are righteous, NOT some righteous vs. some unrighteous
- "**the God of Israel**" - provides background about Yahweh, NOT distinguishing from another god
- "**Ezra, the priest, the scribe**" - reminds readers who Ezra was
- "**worthless idols**" - all idols are worthless, NOT distinguishing from valuable idols
- "**the one rescuing us**" - describes Jesus' role, NOT distinguishing between Jesuses

### figs-distinguish is NOT FOR:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "the holy place" vs. "the most holy place" | Actually distinguishing two different places |
| "the woman who bore him" | Actually distinguishing which woman (his mother from all women) |
| "the scribes having come down from Jerusalem" | Actually distinguishing these scribes from local scribes |
| "angels who had sinned" | Actually distinguishing sinful angels from those who didn't sin |
| Any phrase genuinely narrowing down which specific thing is meant | Legitimate distinction |

## Two Categories of figs-distinguish

### Category 1: Informing/Reminding Phrases (Most Common)
Phrases that add information or remind readers about a known entity:

| Pattern | Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Title/role descriptions | "Ezra, the priest, the scribe" | Reminds readers who Ezra is |
| Background information | "Jerusalem, which is in Judah" | Informs readers where Jerusalem is |
| Character epithets | "Haman, the adversary of the Jews" | Clarifies Haman's role in the story |
| Divine descriptions | "Yahweh, the God of Israel" | Background about who Yahweh is |
| Relational descriptions | "Simon Peter, a servant and apostle of Jesus Christ" | Describes Peter's relationship to Christ |
| Quality descriptions | "your righteous judgments" | ALL God's judgments are righteous |

### Category 2: Phrases That Could Be Misread as Distinguishing
When the language pattern looks like a distinction but isn't:

| Pattern | Example | Why NOT a Distinction |
|---------|---------|----------------------|
| Relative clauses with "who" | "Jesus, the one rescuing us" | Not distinguishing from another Jesus |
| Participial phrases | "the one having given" (describing God) | Not distinguishing from another god |
| Appositional phrases | "our God and Father" | Not two separate entities |
| Attributive adjectives | "worthless idols" | ALL idols are worthless |
| Descriptive epithets | "David, the man of God" | Reminds of David's character |

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify modifying phrases with nouns**: Look for phrases that describe, clarify, or provide information about a noun
   - Relative clauses: "who...", "which...", "that..."
   - Appositional phrases: "Ezra, the priest"
   - Descriptive adjectives: "righteous judgments"
   - Participial phrases: "the one calling you"

2. **Ask: Is this DISTINGUISHING or INFORMING?**
   - DISTINGUISHING: "the scribes who came from Jerusalem" (vs. local scribes)
   - INFORMING: "Noah, a preacher of righteousness" (describing Noah, not distinguishing him)

3. **Key test - Does another option exist?**
   - "your righteous judgments" - Are there unrighteous judgments from God? NO -> INFORMING
   - "angels who sinned" - Are there angels who didn't sin? YES -> DISTINGUISHING
   - "Mordecai, the Jew" - Is there another Mordecai in this story? NO -> INFORMING

4. **Check for background/reminder function**:
   - Is the phrase reminding readers of something already known?
   - Is the phrase providing background for those who might not know?
   - If yes to either, likely figs-distinguish for INFORMING

## Common Patterns in Published Notes

### Pattern 1: Background Information About Persons
**Example**: "Ezra, the priest, the scribe"
**Note**: "Here the book repeats some background information to remind readers who Ezra was. Alternate translation: [Ezra, who was a priest and a scribe]"

### Pattern 2: Background Information About Places
**Example**: "Jerusalem, which is in Judah"
**Note**: "Here, Cyrus provides some background information about the city where he wants the Jews to rebuild the temple of Yahweh, since many of the recipients of his message might not have known where Jerusalem was. Alternate translation: [Jerusalem, the capital city of the province of Judah]"

### Pattern 3: Further Information About God
**Example**: "God, the one having given us the Spirit"
**Note**: "Here Paul is adding more information about **God**. He is not distinguishing between different gods. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use a form that clearly adds information instead of distinguishing between people. Alternate translation: [and he has given]"

### Pattern 4: Clarifying Information
**Example**: "Haman, the adversary of the Jews"
**Note**: "This phrase gives clarifying information about Haman. Alternate translation: [the enemy of the Jews]"

### Pattern 5: Descriptive Phrases Not Making Distinctions
**Example**: "our God and Father"
**Note**: "The phrase **and Father** gives further information about **our God**. It is not making a distinction between **God** and **Father** as if they are two separate entities. If it would be helpful in your language, you could make the relationship between these words more clear. Alternate translation: [our God, who is our Father]"

### Pattern 6: Reminding Readers
**Example**: "who is 90 years old"
**Note**: "The phrase **who is 90 years old** is a reminder of Sarah's age. It tells why Abraham was asking the question. He did not expect that a woman who was that old could bear a child. He was not distinguishing one woman named Sarah from another woman named Sarah who was a different age."

## Examples from Published Notes

### Ezra/Nehemiah (Background Information)
- "Ezra the priest" -> "Here the book repeats some background information to remind readers who Ezra was"
- "Moses, the man of God" -> "Here the book repeats some background information to remind readers who Moses was"
- "David, the king of Israel" -> "Here the book provides some background information to describe more fully who David was"
- "Yahweh, the God of heaven" -> "Here Cyrus provides some background information to inform his subjects who Yahweh is"
- "the Chaldean" (after Nebuchadnezzar) -> "Here the elders provide some further background information about who Nebuchadnezzar was"

### Romans (Clarifying Descriptions)
- "concerning his Son" -> "This phrase gives us further information about 'the gospel of God'"
- "the Creator, who is blessed to eternity" -> "This phrase gives further information about **the Creator**"
- "whom he foreknew" (about God's people) -> "This phrase gives further information about God's **people**"
- "the one having mercy" (describing God) -> "This phrase gives further information about **God**"

### 2 Corinthians (Adding Information About God)
- "the one having given" -> "Here Paul is adding more information about **God**. He is not distinguishing between different gods"
- "the one having reconciled" -> Same pattern
- "the one comforting the humble" -> Same pattern

### 1 Thessalonians (Descriptive Phrases)
- "Jesus, the one rescuing us" -> "The clause **the one rescuing us** describes Jesus in his role as rescuer. Paul is not distinguishing between more than one **Jesus**"
- "the one calling you" (about God) -> "Here Paul is adding more information about God. He is not distinguishing between different gods"

### Esther (Character Epithets)
- "the Jew" (after Mordecai) -> "This phrase gives clarifying information about Mordecai"
- "the adversary of the Jews" (after Haman) -> "This phrase gives clarifying information about Haman"
- "the daughter of Abihail" -> "This information reminds the reader who Esther was"

### Galatians (Clarifying Relationships)
- "our God and Father" -> "The phrase **and Father** gives further information about **our God**. It is not making a distinction"
- "a servant and an apostle of Jesus Christ" -> "This phrase gives further information about Simon Peter"

### Mark (Distinguishing vs. Not)
- "the scribes having come down from Jerusalem" -> ACTUALLY distinguishing (use figs-distinguish to note distinction)
- "one eating with me" -> "Since all the disciples were eating with him, Jesus is not making distinctions between disciples"

## Translation Strategies from TA

1. **Put the information in another part of the sentence** and add words that show its purpose:
   - "I hate those who serve **worthless** idols" -> "**Because idols are worthless**, I hate those who serve them"
   - "your **righteous** judgments are good" -> "your judgments are good **because they are righteous**"

2. **Use your language's way for expressing added information**:
   - "You are my Son, **whom I love**" -> "You are my Son. **I love you** and I am pleased with you"
   - May involve punctuation (parentheses, commas) or voice changes

## Distinguishing figs-distinguish From Other Issues

| If the phrase is... | Use... |
|---------------------|--------|
| Actually making a distinction between items | Note may still use figs-distinguish but explain the distinction |
| Using metaphor ("house" = descendants) | figs-metaphor |
| Using an idiom ("son of valor") | figs-idiom |
| An appositive providing a name | translate-names |
| Providing implicit information | figs-explicit |

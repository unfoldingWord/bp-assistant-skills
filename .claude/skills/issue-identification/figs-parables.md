# figs-parables

## Purpose
Identify parables and brief illustrations that may need translation notes to help readers recognize the literary form.

## Definition
A parable is a short story that teaches something true in a way that is easy to understand and hard to forget. Though the events could happen, they did not actually happen - they are told only to teach the intended lesson(s).

## Key Characteristics
- Short fictional story with realistic events
- Rarely contains specific proper names (helps distinguish from historical accounts)
- Often uses figures of speech (simile, metaphor) within the story
- Purpose: teach spiritual/moral truth through illustration
- May be explicitly introduced ("he told them a parable") or simply begin with the story

## NOT figs-parables (Use These Instead)

| Content | Use Instead | Reason |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Single metaphor within normal speech | figs-metaphor | Not a story structure |
| Single simile ("like a lion") | figs-simile | Comparison, not narrative |
| Proverbs/sayings | writing-proverbs | Different form |
| Actual historical events | No note | Real events, not illustrations |
| Visions with symbolic imagery | writing-symlanguage | Symbolic images, not teaching stories |

## Recognition Patterns

### Explicit Markers
- "He spoke a parable to them"
- "He told them this parable/story"
- "Learn the parable from..."
- "The kingdom of heaven is like..."
- "The kingdom of God is compared to..."
- "It is like..." (in teaching context)

### Implicit Parables (No Introduction)
Jesus sometimes begins directly with the story:
- "A man had two sons..."
- "There was a rich man who..."
- "A certain man planted a vineyard..."
- "No one lights a lamp and..."

---

## Categories

### Kingdom Parables (Matthew 13, etc.)
"The kingdom of heaven is like..." comparisons teaching about God's rule.
Examples: sower, weeds, mustard seed, yeast, treasure, pearl, net

### Teaching Illustrations
Brief stories making a point about behavior or truth.
Examples: lamp on stand, salt, blind leading blind, strong man

### Character Parables
Stories featuring unnamed characters in relatable situations.
Examples: good Samaritan, prodigal son, unforgiving servant, workers in vineyard

### OT Parables (Rare)
Characters in OT telling illustrative stories.
Example: Jotham's parable of the trees (Judges 9:8-15)

### Johannine "Parables"
John uses Greek word that means "parable" for extended metaphors.
Examples: shepherd and sheep (John 10), the vine (John 15)

---

## Note Purpose

figs-parables notes help readers:
1. Recognize the text as a teaching illustration (not literal history)
2. Understand the teaching purpose
3. Know how to introduce stories naturally in their language

---

## Common Note Patterns

### Introducing a Parable
"To teach [audience], Jesus offers a story or illustration. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could indicate that explicitly."
AT: [Listen to this story: ...]

### Introducing an Illustration
"To help [audience] understand what he has been teaching, Jesus provides an illustration."
AT: [Then Jesus gave them this illustration: ...]

### When Story Begins Abruptly
"Jesus tells a short story that continues through [verse]. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could indicate that explicitly."
AT: [Here is an illustration about this: ...]

## Recognition Process

1. **Check for explicit parable markers**: Does text say "parable" or "told them this story"?

2. **Check for kingdom comparisons**: "Kingdom of heaven/God is like..."?

3. **Identify teaching context**: Is speaker using a story to make a point?

4. **Verify story structure**: Does it have narrative elements (characters, actions, outcome)?

5. **Distinguish from history**: Are there proper names? Could this be a real event being recounted?

6. **Check audience and purpose**: Who is being taught? What is the lesson?

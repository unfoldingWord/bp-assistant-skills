# figs-declarative

## Purpose
Identify cases where declarative statements are used for purposes other than giving information, including commands, requests, and performatives.

## Definition
A declarative statement normally gives information about how things are or will be. However, in biblical literature, statements are sometimes used for other purposes:

1. **Commands/Instructions** - Future tense used as an obligation ("you will/shall do X" = "you must do X")
2. **Requests** - A statement expressing ability as a request ("you are able to heal me" = "please heal me")
3. **Performatives** - A statement that causes something to happen ("cursed is the ground" = God curses it)

## NOT figs-declarative (Use These Instead)

### Use figs-imperative for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| "Give us our bread" (prayer) | Imperative form for polite request |
| "Do not be afraid" | Imperative form for encouragement |
| "Be clean" (Jesus healing) | Imperative form causing healing |

### Key distinction: figs-declarative vs. figs-imperative
- **figs-declarative**: The FORM is a statement (indicative), but the FUNCTION is command/request
- **figs-imperative**: The FORM is a command (imperative), but the FUNCTION is something else (request, condition, etc.)

### Use translate-tense for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Past tense for future events | Prophetic past (figs-pastforfuture is a subset) |
| Present for future | Showing certainty |

### When NO note is needed:
- ULT uses "shall" for Hebrew imperfect expressing obligation - addressed in book intro instead
- Regular statements giving information
- Regular predictions about the future

## Categories with Examples

### 1. Future Form as Command/Instruction (Most Common)
When a future tense ("you will/shall") expresses what someone SHOULD or MUST do.

**Markers:**
- Future tense verb form in original language
- Context shows obligation or instruction, not prediction
- Often in law, teachings, or commands from God

**Examples from processed notes:**
- "You will love your neighbor as yourself" (Lev 19:18, Matt 22:39, Mark 12:31, Rom 13:9, Jas 2:8)
- "You will be holy" (1 Pet 1:16)
- "You will not muzzle an ox" (1 Tim 5:18)
- "he will be last of all" (Mark 9:35) - Jesus giving instruction
- "whoever wants to become great among you will be your servant" (Mark 10:43-44)

### 2. Statement as Request
When a declarative statement functions as a polite request.

**Markers:**
- Statement about someone's ability or willingness
- Speaker clearly wants the person to act
- Often in appeals to God or superiors

**Examples from processed notes:**
- "Lord, if you are willing, you are able to make me clean" (Matt 8:2, Mark 1:40) - leper asking Jesus
- "to which you do well to pay attention" (2 Pet 1:19) - = you should pay attention

### 3. Performatives
When stating something causes it to happen.

**Markers:**
- Speaker is God, Jesus, or someone with authority
- The statement itself performs the action
- Often blessings, curses, or forgiveness

**Examples from processed notes:**
- "Cursed is the ground because of you" (Gen 3:17) - God curses by stating
- "Son, your sins are forgiven" (Mark 2:5) - Jesus forgives by stating
- "Blessed is the God and Father" (1 Pet 1:3) - = Let us bless/praise

### 4. Statement as Exhortation
When a statement functions to encourage or urge the audience.

**Markers:**
- Blessing formula or praise statement
- Speaker is urging audience to join in praise
- Often begins "Blessed is..."

**Examples from processed notes:**
- "Blessed is the God and Father" (1 Pet 1:3) - = Let us bless/praise God

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify statement form**: Is there an indicative/declarative sentence (not imperative or question)?

2. **Check if it gives information**: Is it simply describing a fact or making a prediction?
   - If yes: NO NOTE NEEDED
   - If no: Continue to step 3

3. **Determine the function**:

   | Question | If YES | Type |
   |----------|--------|------|
   | Is future tense used for obligation? | Command/Instruction | figs-declarative |
   | Is a statement about ability really a request? | Request | figs-declarative |
   | Does stating it make it happen? | Performative | figs-declarative |
   | Is speaker urging praise/blessing? | Exhortation | figs-declarative |
   | Is speaker expressing a wish? | Wish/Desire | figs-declarative |

4. **Check existing patterns**: Look for similar expressions in processed notes

## Critical Distinctions

### figs-declarative vs. figs-imperative:
- "You will love your neighbor" (future statement) -> **figs-declarative** (statement as command)
- "Love your neighbor" (imperative) -> **NO NOTE** (regular command, understood)
- "Give us our bread" (imperative as request) -> **figs-imperative** (command form as request)

### figs-declarative vs. translate-tense:
- "You will love" (instruction) -> **figs-declarative** (function is command)
- "He gave" for future (prophetic past) -> **translate-tense** (tense issue, not function)

### ULT "shall" - Special Case:
Per Issues Resolved: When ULT uses "shall" to translate Hebrew imperfect expressing obligation:
- Discuss in **book introduction** rather than writing individual figs-declarative notes
- First instance points back to intro
- Do NOT write figs-declarative for every "shall"

However, when NT writers quote LXX with future tense translating Hebrew "shall" forms:
- ULT says "will" (not "shall") in NT quotation
- Write **figs-declarative** note even if OT source says "shall"

### When NO note is needed:
- Regular statements giving information
- Regular predictions about the future
- ULT "shall" for obligations (handle in book intro instead)

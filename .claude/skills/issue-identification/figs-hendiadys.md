# figs-hendiadys

## Purpose
Identify hendiadys (two different words joined by "and" expressing a single idea where one modifies the other).

## Definition
In a hendiadys, a speaker uses two words that mean **different things**, connected with "and," to express a **single idea**. Usually one word is the primary idea and the other word further describes it (like an adjective or adverb modifying).

Key insight: **One word modifies the other** - this is NOT two equal synonyms (that would be doublet).

---

## Critical Distinction: Hendiadys vs Doublet

| Hendiadys | Doublet |
|-----------|---------|
| Words mean **different things** | Words mean **the same thing** |
| One **modifies** the other | Words are **equal synonyms** |
| "kingdom and glory" = glorious kingdom | "scattered and dispersed" = spread out |
| "answered and said" = responded | "blameless and righteous" = very righteous |

**Test**:
- Can you make one word an adjective/adverb modifying the other? -> Hendiadys
- Can you collapse both to one intensified word (they're synonyms)? -> Doublet

---

## Hendiadys Categories

### 1. Manner Hendiadys (Verb + Verb)
One verb tells HOW the other action was done:

| Expression | Meaning | Pattern |
|------------|---------|---------|
| "answered and said" | responded | Very common in Luke; "answered" tells manner |
| "hurried and came" | came hurriedly | "hurried" modifies "came" |
| "exclaimed and said" | said excitedly | "exclaimed" tells manner |
| "going and growing" | continued to grow | "going" = manner of growth |
| "sent and called" | **Careful**: if synonymous = doublet |

**"Answered and said"**: This is extremely common (65+ times in Luke alone). The words together mean the person responded. Per Issues Resolved, address with writing-quotations when it functions as a quote margin.

### 2. Descriptive Hendiadys (Noun + Noun)
One noun describes what kind the other is:

| Expression | Meaning | Pattern |
|------------|---------|---------|
| "kingdom and glory" | glorious kingdom | "glory" describes the kingdom |
| "spirit and power of Elijah" | powerful spirit of Elijah | "power" describes spirit |
| "mouth and wisdom" | wise words | "wisdom" describes what mouth produces |
| "thunder and rain" | thunderstorm | "thunder" describes type of rain |
| "God and Father" | God who is Father | "Father" identifies who God is |
| "waste and desolation" | desolate wasteland | "desolation" describes waste |

### 3. Adjective Hendiadys (Adj + Adj)
One adjective modifies the other (not equal synonyms):

| Expression | Meaning | Pattern |
|------------|---------|---------|
| "willing and obedient" | willingly obedient | "willing" modifies "obedient" |
| "youth and good" | good-looking young man | "good" describes the youth |
| "teaches and deceives" | deceptively teaches | "deceives" describes manner |

### 4. Single Referent Hendiadys
Two phrases that refer to the same person, thing, or event:

| Expression | Meaning | Pattern |
|------------|---------|---------|
| "our great God and Savior Jesus Christ" | One person: Jesus | Two titles, one referent |
| "the blessed hope and appearing of the glory" | One event: Christ's return | Two descriptions, one event |
| "to God and to his throne" | God's throne | Two phrases, one destination |

---

## Recognition Process

1. **Look for two words joined by "and"**

2. **Check if they mean different things**:
   - If synonyms (same meaning) -> check figs-doublet instead
   - If different meanings -> continue

3. **Test the modification relationship**:
   - Can one become an adjective/adverb modifying the other?
   - "kingdom and glory" -> "glorious kingdom" (YES = hendiadys)
   - Does one describe the manner/type/quality of the other?

4. **Identify the pattern**:
   - Verb + Verb (manner): "answered and said"
   - Noun + Noun (descriptive): "spirit and power"
   - Adj + Adj (modifying): "willing and obedient"
   - Single referent: Two phrases for one thing

5. **Verify single idea**:
   - Do the two words together express ONE concept, not two?

---

## NOT figs-hendiadys

### Use figs-doublet when:
Both words are synonyms (equal meaning, not modifying):
- "scattered and dispersed" = both mean spread out
- "blameless and righteous" = both describe moral quality
- "false and deceptive" = both mean dishonest

### Use writing-quotations when:
"Answered and said" functions as a quote margin (speech tag):
- Per Issues Resolved: Address these with writing-quotations when introducing direct speech

### Use figs-parallelism when:
Two full clauses (with subjects and verbs) express the same idea

---

## Translation Strategies from TA

If hendiadys is not natural in target language:

1. **Substitute describing noun with adjective**:
   - "kingdom and glory" -> "glorious kingdom"

2. **Substitute describing noun with phrase**:
   - "mouth and wisdom" -> "words of wisdom"

3. **Substitute describing adjective with adverb**:
   - "willing and obedient" -> "willingly obedient"

4. **Clarify single referent**:
   - "our great God and Savior Jesus Christ" -> "Jesus Christ, who is our great God and Savior"

5. **For manner verbs, use adverb**:
   - "hurried and came" -> "came quickly"
   - "exclaimed and said" -> "exclaimed"

## Common Patterns

### "Answered and said" Pattern
Very common in biblical narrative. Options:
- If quote margin: writing-quotations (AT: "responded")
- If not responding to speech but to situation: figs-hendiadys + figs-explicit to clarify

### "X and Y" where Y describes X
- Test: Can Y become an adjective for X?
- "spirit and power" -> "powerful spirit"
- "thunder and rain" -> "thunderstorm"

### Two titles for one person
- "God and Father" -> "God, who is Father"
- "Lord and Savior" -> Check if one person or two

---

## Why This Is a Translation Issue

Many languages do not use hendiadys. Readers may:
- Think two separate things are meant, not one
- Not understand the modifying relationship
- Miss that phrases refer to a single person/event

# translate-transliterate

## Purpose
Identify when a Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek word should be transliterated rather than translated.

## Definition
Transliteration is copying a word from one language into another by representing each letter with the corresponding letter in the target script. This preserves the sound of the original word rather than its meaning.

## When to Use translate-transliterate

Use this issue for **non-name words** that the ULT keeps in their original language form:
- Hebrew/Aramaic words that carry theological or cultural significance (Amen, Abba, Hosanna)
- Terms without clear English equivalents (Urim and Thummim, ephod, cherubim)
- Words where both the sound AND meaning matter (Maranatha, Hallelu Yah)
- Musical/technical terms (Selah, maskil)
- Place names where the meaning is also significant (Valley of Achor = "trouble")

## NOT translate-transliterate

Per Issues Resolved (final authority):

| Situation | Use Instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| Proper names where translators choose transliteration vs. meaning (Areopagus vs. Mars Hill) | translate-names |
| Standard biblical names (David, Jerusalem, Babylon) | translate-names |
| Unknown objects/creatures (fig tree, seraph) | translate-unknown |
| Words in unfoldingWord Translation Words | Usually no note needed |

**Key distinction**: translate-names for NAMES where meaning could be expressed; translate-transliterate for other WORDS that ULT has already transliterated.

## Categories

### 1. Liturgical/Religious Terms
Words used in worship that may be kept for their sacred resonance:
- **Amen** - affirmation ("so be it")
- **Hosanna** - praise/plea ("save!")
- **Hallelu Yah** - praise command ("praise Yah")
- **Abba** - intimate address for father
- **Maranatha** - Aramaic prayer ("Lord, come!")

### 2. Untranslatable Objects/Concepts
Items with no clear equivalent:
- **Urim and Thummim** - priestly decision objects
- **ephod** - priestly garment
- **cherubim** - heavenly beings

### 3. Terms with Sound AND Meaning
Words where both are important:
- **Boanerges** - nickname Jesus gave ("Sons of Thunder")
- **Golgotha** - place name ("Place of the Skull")
- **Ephphatha** - Aramaic healing word ("Be opened")
- **Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani** - Jesus' cry from the cross

### 4. Place Names with Significant Meaning
Locations named for events or characteristics:
- **Valley of Achor** ("trouble") - named after Achan's sin
- **En-Hakkore** ("Spring of the Caller") - Samson's naming
- **Valley of Jehoshaphat** ("Yahweh judges")
- **Havvoth Jair** ("Cities of Jair")

### 5. Titles and Epithets
Divine titles kept in original form:
- **El Shaddai** - divine title ("God Almighty")
- **Hades** - Greek abode of the dead

## Recognition Pattern

1. ULT contains a word in Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek script OR spelled phonetically
2. Word is NOT a standard proper name (person/place with no significant meaning)
3. Word carries meaning that translators might want to express
4. Context often includes an explanation of the word's meaning

## Note Pattern

For words the biblical author translated:
> The word **[WORD]** is a [Hebrew/Aramaic/Greek] word. [AUTHOR] has spelled it out using [Greek/English] letters so readers would know how it sounded. Since [AUTHOR] states what this word means [in this verse/later in the verse], you also should spell it out the way it sounds in your language.

For words needing translator decision:
> The word **[WORD]** is a [Hebrew/Aramaic] word. You will need to decide if you will use this word in its [Hebrew/Aramaic] form or if you will instead translate the meaning into your language. Either approach has broad support. If you use the [Hebrew/Aramaic] word, you could spell it the way it sounds in your language and then put the translation in a footnote. Alternate translation: [meaning]

## Special Cases

### Hallelu Yah
Per Issues Resolved: ULT says "Hallelu Yah" with translate-transliterate note offering AT [Praise Yah] AND a translate-names note explaining Yah is a shortened form of Yahweh.

### Musical Terms in Psalms
Per Issues Resolved: Retain Hebrew musical terms (Shoshannim, Sheminith) with translate-transliterate notes suggesting either transliteration or expressing the probable meaning.

### Selah
Per Issues Resolved: Represent "Selah" in both ULT and UST wherever it appears.

# translate-plural

## Purpose
Identify mismatches between grammatical number and conceptual number in Hebrew (and occasionally Greek) - either plurals referring to singular concepts OR singulars referring to plural/collective concepts.

## Definition
Biblical languages sometimes use grammatical number that differs from the conceptual number:

**Plural for Singular:**
1. Intensive/plural of excellence - emphasizing greatness or intensity
2. Royal importance (kings referring to themselves as "we/us")
3. Authorial humility (writers avoiding direct self-reference)

**Singular for Plural:**
4. Representative singular - one individual standing for a group/category (common in poetry when describing enemies, the wicked, etc.)

---

## Categories

### 1. Intensive/Plural of Excellence
A plural form emphasizing the greatness, vastness, or complexity of a singular concept.

| ULT Text | Concept | What's Emphasized |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| "seas" (Jonah 2:3) | the sea | vastness or turbulence |
| "securities" (Job 12:6) | security | completeness of security |
| "heights" (Job 16:19, 25:2, 31:2) | heaven | supreme realm |
| "strengths" (Job 41:12) | strength | great strength |
| "breadths" (Job 38:18) | earth's breadth | vastness |
| "slumbers" (Job 33:15) | slumber | deep/sound slumber |
| "oppressions" (Job 35:9) | oppression | severity |
| "rains" (Job 37:6) | rain | heaviness |
| "golds/silvers" (Job 22:25) | precious metals | supreme quality |
| "vengeances" (Judg 11:36) | vengeance | great vengeance |
| "enemies" (Judg 16:23) | enemy (Samson) | emphasis on one enemy |

**From Issues Resolved**: For translate-plural notes addressing the "plural of excellence" or "intensive plural," do not say simply that the speaker is using that form for emphasis. Say **what** is being emphasized, e.g., "Jonah is using the plural **seas** to emphasize the greatness or complexity of the sea."

### 2. Royal "We" (Majestic Plural)
A king or leader referring to themselves using plural pronouns to show importance or represent their people.

| Example | Speaker | Context |
|---------|---------|---------|
| "the letter that you sent to **us**" (Ezra 4:18) | King Artaxerxes | Responding as king |

### 3. Authorial "We" (Humility)
An author using plural pronouns to avoid direct self-reference, often in epistles.

| Example | Speaker | Context |
|---------|---------|---------|
| "through whom **we** received grace" (Rom 1:5) | Paul | Letter from Paul alone |

### 4. Idiomatic Plural for Time
Hebrew uses plural "days" to mean "year" in certain expressions.

| ULT Pattern | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| "from days to days" (1 Sam 1:3, 2:19) | from year to year |
| "sacrifice of the days" (1 Sam 1:21, 20:6) | yearly/annual sacrifice |
| "was days" (1 Sam 27:7) | one year |
| "for the days" (Judg 17:10) | each year |

### 5. Plural Form for Deity (Special Case)
The Hebrew word for God (elohim) has plural form but usually singular meaning.

| Context | Meaning | Note Type |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| "gods" (Judg 9:9, 9:13) | Could be plural gods OR singular God | translate-plural with options |

### 6. Representative Singular (Singular for Plural)
A singular pronoun or noun used to represent a group or category. Common in Hebrew poetry when describing enemies, the wicked, or types of people.

| ULT Text | Referent | AT |
|----------|----------|-----|
| "when **he** treads his arrows" (Ps 58:7) | the wicked (plural group) | "when **they** tread their arrows" |
| "the wicked **man**" | wicked people as a category | "wicked **people**" |
| "**his** tongue" (describing enemies) | their tongues | "**their** tongues" |

**Recognition cues:**
- Singular pronoun where context established a plural group (e.g., "the wicked" then "he")
- Poetic descriptions of enemies/evildoers switching between plural and singular
- Wisdom literature describing typical behaviors using singular representative

**Note pattern:** The psalmist/author is using a singular form to represent [the wicked/enemies/etc.] as a group. If it would be more natural in your language, you could use a plural form. Alternate translation: [plural version]

## NOT translate-plural

### Simple Count Plurals
When a plural refers to multiple actual items:
- "shields" because the army has multiple shields -> NOT translate-plural
- "houses" referring to many buildings -> NOT translate-plural

### Collective Nouns
Singular nouns referring to groups:
- "Israel" referring to all Israelites -> grammar-collectivenouns
- "flock" referring to many sheep -> grammar-collectivenouns

### Generic Nouns (Timeless Truths)
Singular nouns representing a category in proverbial/timeless statements:
- "the ant" representing ants in general -> figs-genericnoun
- "Blessed is the man who..." -> figs-genericnoun

**Distinction from representative singular:** figs-genericnoun is for timeless wisdom statements about a type. Representative singular (translate-plural) is when a singular suddenly represents a specific plural group established in context (e.g., "the wicked" in v.3 becomes "he" in v.7).

### English Style Adjustments
When English naturally uses singular where Hebrew uses literal plural:
- Matt 15:19 lists "murders, adulteries, sexual immoralities" - English may prefer singular forms, but this is style not intensive plural

## Recognition Process

### For Plural-for-Singular:

1. **Identify unexpected plural**: Is a plural form used where you'd expect singular?

2. **Check the context**:
   - Is this one object/concept/person described with plural?
   - Does the plural seem to emphasize greatness, intensity, or completeness?

3. **Determine the category**:
   - Abstract quality/nature -> intensive plural
   - Pronoun from royalty -> royal we
   - Pronoun from author -> authorial we
   - "days" meaning year -> idiomatic time expression

4. **Identify what's being emphasized**: Before writing a note, determine specifically what aspect is intensified (greatness, vastness, completeness, severity, etc.)

### For Singular-for-Plural (Representative Singular):

1. **Identify number mismatch**: Is a singular pronoun/noun used where context established a plural group?

2. **Check the context**:
   - Was a plural group mentioned earlier (the wicked, enemies, evildoers)?
   - Does "he/his/him" now refer back to that group?

3. **Distinguish from figs-genericnoun**:
   - Generic noun: timeless truth about a type ("Blessed is the man...")
   - Representative singular: specific plural group in context becomes singular ("the wicked... he")

## Common AT Patterns

| Source Pattern | AT Options |
|----------------|------------|
| Intensive plural noun | "[great/vast/deep/complete] [singular]" |
| Royal "we/us" | "me/I" |
| Authorial "we" | "I" |
| "days to days" | "year to year", "annually", "each year" |
| Plural form of God | "[God]" or "[gods]" depending on context |
| Representative singular "he/his" | "they/their" (matching the plural group in context) |

## Genre Considerations

### Wisdom/Poetry (Job, Psalms, Proverbs)
- Intensive plurals common - Hebrew poetry often uses this device for emphasis
- **Representative singular very common** - descriptions of enemies/wicked often switch between plural group and singular representative ("the wicked... when he...")

### Narrative (Samuel, Kings, Chronicles)
Royal "we" appears in speeches by kings. Time expressions ("days" = year) are common.

### Prophets (Jonah, etc.)
Intensive plurals emphasize the magnitude of divine action or natural phenomena.

### Epistles (NT)
Authorial "we" appears when a single author uses plural self-reference.

# figs-aside

## Purpose
Identify asides in biblical text where a speaker pauses from addressing one audience to briefly address someone else or themselves.

## Definition
An aside is a figure of speech in which someone who is speaking to a person or group pauses to speak confidentially to himself or someone else about those to whom he had been speaking. The speaker does this to indicate in a strong way his thoughts or feelings about that person or group.

## Why This Is a Translation Issue
Many languages do not use asides, and readers could be confused by them. They may wonder why the speaker suddenly starts talking to himself or someone else about the people he is speaking with.

From Issues Resolved and published translation notes:

| Expression | Context | Notes |
|------------|---------|-------|
| "Remember me, my God" | Nehemiah addressing readers, turns to God | Prayer aside - use quotation marks |
| "There is no understanding in him" | Yahweh addressing Edom, speaks about Edom to himself | Self-commentary aside |
| "I speak according to men" | Paul showing he's not challenging God | Parenthetical clarification |
| "whose judgment is just" | Paul about false accusers | Parenthetical aside about opponents |

## NOT figs-aside (Use These Instead)

### Use figs-apostrophe for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Addressing absent/dead person | Apostrophe is addressing someone who cannot respond; aside is ABOUT those being addressed |
| Addressing personified thing | Speaking TO the thing, not about current audience |

### Key Distinction from figs-apostrophe:
- **figs-aside**: Speaker turns away from audience to speak about them (to self, to God, to reader)
- **figs-apostrophe**: Speaker addresses someone/something that cannot hear or respond

### Use writing-quotations for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Simple quote margin/speech tag | Just introducing direct speech, not shifting addressee |

### Use figs-explicit for:
| Expression | Reason |
|------------|--------|
| Narrator providing background | If not interrupting a speech, it's just implicit information |

## Aside Categories with Examples

### 1. Prayer Asides (Speaker turns from audience to God)
Most common in Nehemiah:
- **Nehemiah 4:4** - Nehemiah addressing readers, turns to pray "Remember me, my God"
- **Nehemiah 5:19** - Same pattern
- **Nehemiah 13:14, 22, 31** - Same pattern

**Recognition markers:**
- Direct address to God ("my God")
- Imperative verb ("Remember me")
- Interrupts narrative addressed to readers
- Often brief (single sentence)

**Translation strategy:** Put prayer in quotation marks to indicate shift

### 2. Self-Commentary Asides (Speaker speaks about audience to self)
- **Obadiah 1:7** - Yahweh addressing Edom, then says "There is no understanding in him" about Edom

**Recognition markers:**
- Shift from second person ("you") to third person ("him/them")
- Comment evaluating or judging the addressee
- Brief editorial comment

**Translation strategy:** Make clear this is speaker's thought about addressee

### 3. Internal Thought Asides (Character thinks to self)
- **Exodus 2:14** - Moses says/thinks "Certainly the thing is known" to himself

**Recognition markers:**
- Narrator uses "said" but content is internal reflection
- Character is not speaking to anyone present
- Often introduced with "and said" but no audience specified

**Translation strategy:** Introduce as thought rather than speech

### 4. Parenthetical Clarification Asides (Author/narrator explains)
- **Romans 3:5** - "I speak according to men" (Paul clarifying his rhetorical approach)
- **Romans 3:8** - "whose judgment is just" (Paul's aside about accusers)
- **Ezra 4:11** - "This is a copy of the letter" (narrator interrupting quotation)

**Recognition markers:**
- Interrupts flow of argument or narrative
- Addresses reader directly
- Provides meta-commentary on what's being said
- Often could be placed in parentheses

**Translation strategy:** Use parentheses or natural aside marker in your language

## Recognition Process

1. **Identify shift in addressee**: Does the speaker suddenly stop addressing one person/group and address someone else?

2. **Determine the aside type**:
   - Is speaker turning to address God? --> Prayer aside
   - Is speaker commenting about addressee to self? --> Self-commentary aside
   - Is character thinking rather than speaking? --> Internal thought aside
   - Is author/narrator clarifying for reader? --> Parenthetical clarification aside

3. **Verify it's not something else**:
   - Is speaker addressing someone who cannot respond? --> Check figs-apostrophe
   - Is this just background information? --> Check figs-explicit
   - Is this just a quote margin? --> Check writing-quotations

4. **Note the relationship**: What is the aside ABOUT? (Usually about the original addressee)

---

## Biblical Book Patterns

### Books with frequent asides:
- **Nehemiah** - Multiple prayer asides to God (4:4, 5:19, 13:14, 13:22, 13:31)
- **Romans** - Parenthetical asides in argumentation (3:5, 3:8)
- **Prophets** - Yahweh commenting about those he addresses (Obadiah 1:7)

### Context clues:
- First-person narration (Nehemiah, Ezra) often includes prayer asides
- Prophetic speeches may include divine self-commentary
- Epistles may include rhetorical clarifications

## Examples from Published Notes

### Nehemiah 5:19
> "Remember me, my God, for good"
>
> As in 4:4, here Nehemiah speaks directly to God. You could indicate this by putting this verse in quotation marks. Review the note at 4:4 about asides like this if that would be helpful.

### Exodus 2:14
> "and said"
>
> **Certainly the thing is known** was likely something Moses said to himself, that is, he thought it to himself rather than speaking to the men in front of him. If this would be confusing in your language, you might introduce it as a thought. Alternate translation: [and thought]

### Romans 3:5
> "I speak according to men"
>
> Paul could be saying this as an aside in order to show that he is not trying to challenge **the righteousness of God**. If this would be confusing in your language, you could add parentheses, as seen in the ULT, or use a natural way in your language to indicate an aside.
